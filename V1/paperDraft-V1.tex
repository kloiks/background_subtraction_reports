\begin{synopsis}
A quantitative framework is presented for evaluating and optimizing background subtraction methods for synchrotron X-ray fiber diffraction patterns from striated muscle.
\end{synopsis}


%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\begin{abstract}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Accurate background subtraction is a critical preprocessing step in the analysis of X-ray fiber diffraction patterns from striated muscle, where diffuse scattering, instrumental artifacts, and specimen-dependent features complicate the separation of signal and background. Existing background subtraction methods are often applied heuristically, with limited guidance on parameter selection or objective evaluation, leading to variability in downstream quantitative analyses.

In this work, we present a general framework for the systematic evaluation and optimization of background subtraction methods for muscle fiber diffraction images. The framework combines physically motivated image-quality metrics with a global optimization strategy to identify method-specific parameter settings that best balance background suppression and signal preservation. Multiple commonly used background subtraction approaches are evaluated within a unified pipeline, enabling direct, quantitative comparison across datasets.

We apply the framework to synchrotron X-ray fiber diffraction patterns from striated muscle acquired under varied experimental conditions. Results show that optimized parameter selection substantially improves background removal consistency and reduces artifacts relative to default or ad hoc settings, while preserving key diffraction features such as equatorial reflections and layer lines. The proposed metrics correlate well with expert visual assessment and highlight characteristic failure modes of different subtraction strategies.

This framework provides an objective, reproducible basis for selecting and tuning background subtraction methods in muscle fiber diffraction studies and can be extended to other classes of fiber diffraction data. By formalizing evaluation and optimization, it supports more reliable quantitative analysis of synchrotron diffraction experiments.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Reliable background subtraction is essential for quantitative analysis of muscle fiber diffraction patterns, but is challenging due to strong, non-uniform scattering that can obscure weak diffraction features. In this section we motivate the problem, summarize limitations of current approaches, and outline the contributions of the proposed framework.


\subsection{Motivation}

Scientific imaging techniques used in structural biology and materials science that rely on diffraction analysis often face a common challenge: extracting weak diffraction signals from images dominated by complex, non-uniform backgrounds. Such backgrounds arise from multiple sources, including amorphous regions within the sample, lattice disorder, instrumental artifacts, and scattering from the experimental environment. Together, these contributions can obscure subtle diffraction features that are critical for quantitative analysis.

In small-angle X-ray fiber diffraction experiments on muscle fibers, the signal of interest consists of diffraction peaks of varying intensity superimposed on a strong, spatially non-uniform diffuse background that must be removed prior to analysis~\cite{ResearchPlanMusico}. X-ray fiber diffraction provides unique insight into the sarcomere-level molecular structure of muscle and has proven valuable for studying the structural consequences of cardiac and skeletal myopathies. Analysis of muscle diffraction patterns can therefore contribute to understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying diseases ranging from nemaline myopathy to heart failure~\cite{MaIrving2022}. Accurate interpretation of these diffraction patterns requires reliable intensity measurements and effective background subtraction.

The background in muscle fiber diffraction patterns consists of diffuse scattering from unstructured material in the sample combined with a pronounced equatorial streak. This streak arises from aligned cylindrical structures within muscle fibers, namely the actin-containing thin filaments and the myosin-containing thick filaments. Although background removal is a general challenge in diffraction imaging, it is particularly difficult in muscle fiber diffraction due to the coexistence of multiple structural components, the large fraction of non-coherently diffracting material that produces strong amorphous scattering, and the presence of intense, anisotropic features such as the equatorial streak.

Analysis of two-dimensional fiber diffraction patterns from partially crystalline structures such as muscle fibers using standard crystallographic techniques is challenging. In addition to diffuse background scattering, muscle diffraction patterns exhibit continuous bands of intensity along the layer lines and complex meridional reflection patterns arising from multiple co-existing structures within the sample. As a result, fiber diffraction data are commonly analyzed using one-dimensional projections following background removal and parametric peak evaluation. Such one-dimensional analyses are effective for sharp reflections along the meridional and equatorial axes, where a large fraction of the signal is concentrated. However, they are less suitable for studying continuous features such as layer lines, where distinguishing diffraction signal from background is inherently difficult.

Even for sharp diffraction spots, valuable information related to lattice ordering and structural heterogeneity is often encoded in the two-dimensional shape of the reflections. This information is largely lost when data are reduced to one-dimensional projections. A robust approach to background removal applied directly to two-dimensional diffraction patterns would therefore enable more comprehensive extraction of structural information, including features that are not typically analyzed systematically.

Existing two-dimensional background subtraction methods commonly rely on non-parametric techniques, such as morphological filtering or iterative smoothing. These approaches typically require manual parameter selection and may inadvertently remove weak diffraction signals. Because the intensity of the background often exceeds that of the signal by orders of magnitude, even small errors in background subtraction can significantly affect downstream quantitative analysis. An objective, physics-informed framework for evaluating background subtraction performance would facilitate systematic comparison of existing methods and support the development of improved approaches. More broadly, reliable separation of weak signals from complex backgrounds has the potential to enhance data fidelity across a wide range of scientific imaging applications.

\subsection{Problem Statement}

The separation of signal from background is a fundamental problem in quantitative imaging. In current approaches to background removal for muscle fiber diffraction patterns, operator intervention is typically required to visually assess subtraction quality and to manually adjust algorithm parameters. Such manual processing introduces several challenges. First, weak diffraction signals may be lost due to the large intensity contrast between background and peaks, combined with the presence of noise that can mask low-intensity features. Second, reliance on visual assessment introduces subjectivity, leading to solutions that may be plausible but not optimal. Finally, reproducibility depends on careful documentation and reuse of user-selected parameters.

The absence of a standardized, quantitative evaluation framework limits the ability to objectively compare background subtraction algorithms and impedes systematic parameter optimization. This lack of objective assessment hinders reproducibility and slows methodological progress.

\subsection{Scope}

This work presents a framework for improving the analysis of weak diffraction signals in the presence of complex, non-uniform backgrounds, a challenge common to many scientific imaging domains. The framework is designed to replace subjective visual assessment with quantitative, physics-informed evaluation criteria. The main contributions are as follows:

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{System of Objective Evaluation Measures}. A set of quantitative metrics is derived from physical properties of the signal generation process, here fiber diffraction from muscle. These measures provide a reproducible standard for assessing background subtraction quality and enable unsupervised comparison of algorithms and parameter sets.

\item \textbf{Faint Detail Preservation}. The framework incorporates faint synthetic diffraction features and penalizes their loss during background subtraction, ensuring that weak but physically meaningful signals are preserved.

\item \textbf{Benchmarking Foundation}. The proposed evaluation framework establishes a baseline for systematic comparison of existing and future background subtraction methods, enabling quantitative assessment of methodological improvements.

\item \textbf{Generalizability}. Although developed in the context of muscle fiber diffraction, the framework is based on general principles derived from signal physics and is adaptable to other imaging modalities characterized by complex, non-uniform backgrounds.

\end{enumerate}

The framework addresses the immediate need for improved background subtraction using existing methods

\section{Related Work}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Background removal strategies in diffraction imaging depend strongly on both the dimensionality of the data and the structural characteristics of the sample. In muscle fiber diffraction, background subtraction is commonly performed either on one-dimensional intensity profiles extracted from the data or directly on two-dimensional diffraction patterns. This section reviews representative approaches in both categories and highlights their limitations in the context of biological fiber diffraction.

\subsection{One-dimensional background subtraction}

Background removal in diffraction analysis is traditionally performed on one-dimensional intensity profiles extracted along directions of interest, most commonly the equator and meridian. In standard practice, diffraction peaks are analyzed individually, and background intensity is estimated by averaging signal in regions adjacent to each peak, which is then subtracted from the integrated peak intensity~\cite{NederKorsunskiy2018}. This approach is simple and effective for isolated reflections but depends on careful selection of background regions and does not account for spatial variation in the background.

Another commonly used technique applies the convex hull algorithm to one-dimensional intensity profiles in order to separate diffraction peaks from a smoothly varying background. While robust to large-scale intensity variations, the convex hull method does not explicitly model background noise and can introduce systematic errors in quantitative measurements.

Parametric background models are also widely used in one-dimensional analyses. Low-order polynomials, Chebyshev polynomials, or spline functions are commonly employed to represent slowly varying backgrounds, while exponential components may be introduced to model steeper background variations~\cite{rajkumar2007fibrefix}. Although these approaches are effective for sharp reflections along the equator and meridian, they inherently discard spatial information contained in the two-dimensional shapes of diffraction features. As a result, one-dimensional methods provide limited access to continuous diffraction features such as layer lines, which contain important structural information.

\subsection{Two-dimensional background subtraction}

Two-dimensional background subtraction methods operate directly on diffraction patterns and aim to preserve spatial information that is lost in one-dimensional projections. Before applying any background subtraction technique, a sample-free measurement is typically subtracted to remove instrumental artifacts and air scattering.

Some two-dimensional approaches rely on explicit assumptions about the geometry or smoothness of the background. Radial symmetry methods assume that the background intensity is symmetric about the beam center and estimate it by averaging pixel intensities over concentric rings~\cite{rajkumar2007fibrefix}. While computationally efficient, this assumption breaks down in the presence of anisotropic background features, such as the equatorial streak characteristic of muscle fiber diffraction. Partition-based methods divide the diffraction pattern into local regions, estimate the background within each region, and interpolate between regions to form a continuous background image. Examples include the roving window method implemented in the CCP13 software suite~\cite{HeEtAl2007} and the radial convex hull approach, which applies one-dimensional convex hull subtraction to angular projections of the pattern~\cite{MuscleXQuadrantFolding}. Although these methods can adapt to spatially varying backgrounds, their performance depends sensitively on partition size and angular resolution and may introduce artifacts in regions with steep background gradients. In particular, the radial convex hull method tends to perform poorly at high scattering angles, where angular sectors span large regions of the detector.

Other methods treat background subtraction as an image processing problem, exploiting differences in spatial scale or frequency content between background and diffraction features. Iterative low-pass filtering assumes that background variations occur at lower spatial frequencies than diffraction peaks and estimates the background through repeated smoothing~\cite{IvanovaMakowski1998}. Its effectiveness depends strongly on the chosen filter scale and the number of iterations, and weak or low-contrast diffraction features may be partially removed. Morphological filtering techniques, such as white top-hat operations~\cite{GonzalezWoodsDIP4e}, estimate the background using a structuring element and subtract it from the original image. Performance depends on the size of the structuring element relative to feature size, and inappropriate choices may suppress extended or low-intensity diffraction signals. Frequency-domain approaches based on Fourier or wavelet transforms separate image components by spatial frequency~\cite{PolarBackground2024, WaveletBiomedOptExpress2021}. These techniques are effective for crystalline diffraction patterns with sharp features but are less suitable for fiber diffraction, where signal and background overlap in frequency space. Kernel-based rolling ball methods~\cite{Sternberg1983} similarly perform well for crystalline samples but are generally less effective for fiber diffraction data.

More recently, data-driven approaches have been explored. Supervised deep learning methods can be trained to perform background subtraction when sufficient labeled data are available~\cite{ArangurenCarmona2020}. However, in diffraction imaging, the lack of ground-truth background and the difficulty of generating representative training datasets limit the practical applicability of such approaches.

Across all two-dimensional methods, the selection of algorithms and parameters typically relies on qualitative visual assessment by domain experts. This reliance introduces subjectivity and increases the risk of suppressing subtle diffraction features. The absence of standardized, quantitative evaluation criteria motivates the development of objective measures for comparing background subtraction methods and guiding systematic parameter optimization.

\section{Methodology}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

This section describes the methodological framework used to evaluate and optimize background subtraction methods for muscle fiber diffraction patterns. We first define qualitative objectives and quantitative metrics that capture physically meaningful properties of successful background removal. To enable objective validation in the absence of ground truth, synthetic diffraction features and region-specific masking are introduced. These metrics are then combined into an aggregate loss function that guides parameter optimization for each background subtraction method. Finally, the experimental design, including algorithm variants and dataset selection, is described.




\subsection{Evaluation Framework}

The evaluation framework is designed to provide objective, physically meaningful criteria for assessing the quality of background subtraction in muscle fiber diffraction images. Because ground-truth background is unavailable for experimental data, the framework combines qualitative expectations derived from physical considerations with quantitative metrics that enable reproducible comparison across methods and parameter settings.

\subsubsection{Qualitative Objectives}

An ideal background-subtracted diffraction image should satisfy several qualitative criteria. True diffraction features should be preserved with minimal distortion and remain clearly distinguishable from the background. The subtraction process should not introduce non-physical artifacts, such as extended negative regions or patterns resembling diffraction features (e.g.\ layer lines or reflection peaks), which would indicate over-subtraction. Since intensity values are non-negative by definition, negative excursions should be minimized. In regions free of diffraction signal, the residual background should be smooth, near zero, and free of structured intensity variations. Finally, for images acquired under similar experimental conditions, background subtraction results and optimal parameters should be consistent across frames. These qualitative objectives motivate the quantitative metrics used below.

\subsubsection{Quantitative Metrics}

Quantitative metrics are defined to capture deviations from the qualitative objectives in a reproducible manner. Several metrics operate directly on the background-subtracted image to assess artifact introduction, background smoothness, and residual baseline behavior.

Oversubtraction is assessed by measuring the fraction of negative-valued pixels that belong to spatially connected clusters larger than a specified minimum size (e.g.\ nine pixels). Random noise typically produces isolated negative pixels, whereas systematic oversubtraction produces spatially connected regions. This metric, denoted $\%\mathrm{Neg}_{\mathrm{con}}$, is ideally zero.

Residual background level is quantified using the share of pixels whose intensity lies within a specified threshold of zero, denoted $\%\mathrm{B}$. This metric reflects how effectively structured background has been removed while preserving natural noise. The threshold is estimated from the average intensity near the edge of the diffraction pattern, where background scattering is expected to be minimal.

Background smoothness is quantified by measuring the average absolute intensity difference between adjacent rows in masked background regions. This smoothness metric, denoted $\mathrm{S}_{\mathrm{BG}}$, captures the presence of structured or oscillatory residual background. It is defined as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{S}_{\mathrm{BG}} =
\frac{1}{N} \sum_i \left| I(y_i + 1) - I(y_i) \right|,
\end{equation}
where the summation is performed over rows within masked background regions to exclude known diffraction features. Smooth residual backgrounds produce low values of this metric.

\subsubsection{Validation Using Synthetic Structures}

Because experimental diffraction images do not provide ground-truth background, synthetic diffraction features are introduced to evaluate preservation of faint signals after background subtraction (see Section~3.2.2). These validation metrics focus on signal loss and oversubtraction behavior.

Preservation of synthetic signal is quantified using the mean squared error between the background-subtracted image and a reference image containing only the injected synthetic features, denoted $\mathrm{MSE}_{\mathrm{syn}}$. Ideally, this value is zero, indicating perfect preservation of synthetic signal. Although masking excludes regions known to contain real diffraction features, partial overlap between synthetic and real structures may increase this metric.

Oversubtraction of synthetic features is further assessed by measuring the fraction of negative-valued pixels remaining after synthetic signal subtraction, denoted $\%\mathrm{Neg}_{\mathrm{syn}}$. This metric emphasizes non-physical negative excursions introduced by background removal and is ideally zero.




\subsection{Synthetic Signal Validation and Masking}

This subsection describes the masking strategy and synthetic signal design used to enable objective evaluation of background subtraction performance in the absence of ground-truth background. Region-specific masking isolates background-dominated areas, while synthetic diffraction features provide controlled test signals for assessing preservation of faint structure.

\subsubsection{Region-Specific Masking}

Accurate calculation of evaluation metrics requires separating regions containing diffraction signal from those dominated by background or artifacts. To achieve this, multiple complementary masks are applied.

Regions containing non-representative or saturated signal are excluded using a main signal mask. In fiber diffraction images, this mask removes the high-intensity central region associated with the direct beam and detector edges. The mask is defined as an annulus with inner radius $r_{\mathrm{min}}$ and outer radius $r_{\mathrm{max}}$.

The strong equatorial background streak characteristic of muscle fiber diffraction is isolated using a dedicated equator background mask. Because the equatorial streak is typically more intense than the surrounding diffuse background, it is treated separately. This mask is implemented as a rectangular region aligned with the equator.

Regions containing synthetic diffraction features are identified using a synthetic feature mask. Around each injected synthetic structure, a rectangular area extending $2\sigma$ in each direction is masked, where $\sigma$ denotes the standard deviation of the synthetic feature.

To avoid contamination from real diffraction signal, regions where genuine diffraction features are expected are excluded using a real feature mask. Layer line regions are masked using horizontal bands centered at known layer line positions. The width of each band is determined by estimating the full width at half maximum (FWHM) of the corresponding layer line using perpendicular intensity projections.

Together, these masks restrict metric evaluation to regions dominated by background, ensuring that evaluation reflects background subtraction quality rather than signal content.

\subsubsection{Synthetic Signal Validation Design}

A central challenge in evaluating background subtraction for diffraction images is the absence of ground-truth background. To address this limitation, synthetic diffraction features resembling real signal are injected into the images prior to background subtraction. Preservation of these features after subtraction provides a controlled measure of signal loss and oversubtraction.

Synthetic features are modeled using two-dimensional Gaussian functions elongated along the horizontal axis, reflecting the typical shape of diffraction features such as layer lines. The synthetic feature is defined as
\begin{equation}
G(x, y) = A \exp\!\left(
-\frac{(x-x_0)^2}{2\sigma_x^2}
-\frac{(y-y_0)^2}{2\sigma_y^2}
\right),
\end{equation}
where $A$ is the amplitude, $(x_0, y_0)$ is the feature center, and $\sigma_x$ and $\sigma_y$ control the horizontal and vertical extent.

The shape parameters are selected automatically for each image to match the scale of real diffraction features. Based on observed correlations in experimental data, $\sigma_x$ and $\sigma_y$ are derived from the average FWHM of prominent reflections, specifically the $I_{1,0}$ equatorial peak and the M1 and M3 meridional reflections. Specifically, $\sigma_x = \frac{I_{1,0}}{5} \times \frac{1}{2\sqrt{2\ln2}}$ and $\sigma_y = \frac{\mathrm{M1}}{20} \times \frac{1}{2\sqrt{2\ln2}}$.

The radial decay of synthetic feature intensity follows a $1/r$ model, where $r$ is the distance from the pattern center. This choice reflects the scattering geometry of X-ray diffraction and approximates the Lorentz correction, which for equatorial and meridional reflections can be expressed as $L \propto 1/\sin 2\theta$~\cite{Buerger1940}. At small scattering angles, $\theta$ is approximately proportional to $r$, making a $1/r$ decay a reasonable approximation~\cite{Denny1995}.

The maximum amplitude of synthetic features is chosen to align, at each radius, with the average intensity of real diffraction features. For each image, the amplitude is scaled relative to the strongest observed reflection, the $I_{1,0}$ equatorial peak. Setting the maximum synthetic feature amplitude to $A_{\mathrm{max}} = 1\%\, I_{1,0}$ ensures that injected features remain faint while spanning a range of scattering angles.

Synthetic features are placed at regular intervals across the image to ensure broad spatial coverage. The sampling frequency $(f_x, f_y)$ controls placement density and is chosen to avoid overlap with known diffraction features. Layer line positions are explicitly accounted for to ensure that synthetic features probe background-dominated regions.

Across datasets and background subtraction methods, the default parameter settings consistently yielded low loss values (Table~\ref{tab:variations}). Although individual datasets exhibit variability in metric values, the relative performance of background subtraction methods remains stable across parameter variations. This indicates that the choice of synthetic signal parameters does not dominate the evaluation outcome.

\subsection{Optimization Procedure}

This subsection describes how the quantitative evaluation metrics are combined into a single objective function and how this objective is used to optimize the parameters of each background subtraction method.



\subsubsection{Aggregate Loss Function}

Background subtraction quality is quantified using a weighted aggregate loss function that combines multiple normalized metrics defined in the evaluation framework. The objective function is constructed to balance physically meaningful constraints, artifact suppression, and preservation of faint diffraction signal. It is defined as
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
L =\;&
w_1\, S_{\mathrm{BG}}'
+ w_2\, \%\mathrm{Neg}_{\mathrm{con}}'
+ w_3\, \%\mathrm{Neg}_{\mathrm{syn}}'
&+ w_4\, \mathrm{MSE}_{\mathrm{syn}}'
+ w_5\, \bigl(1 - \%\mathrm{B}'\bigr)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where each metric is normalized by its average value over the dataset to ensure comparable scale during optimization. A summary of the loss components and their physical interpretation is provided in Table~\ref{tab:loss_terms}.

The weights $w_i$ encode the relative importance of different physical and algorithmic considerations and may be adjusted to reflect user priorities. We initially compare two weighting strategies: equal weighting, with $w_1 : w_2 : w_3 : w_4 : w_5 = 1 : 1 : 1 : 1 : 1$, and domain-informed weighting, with $w_1 : w_2 : w_3 : w_4 : w_5 = 3 : 3 : 2 : 2 : 1$. In the latter case, higher weights are assigned to metrics enforcing direct physical constraints, such as background smoothness and suppression of connected negative regions. Metrics related to synthetic signal preservation receive moderate weight, while the baseline background term is weighted more lightly, as it primarily reflects the degree of overall background cleanup.

Although the optimization is driven by quantitative metrics, visual inspection of residual backgrounds and background-subtracted images remains necessary to identify subtle artifacts that are difficult to capture numerically, such as faint negative excursions or distortion of layer lines. Based on visual comparison, domain-informed weighting consistently produced slightly better results than equal weighting. Further adjustment was therefore applied to emphasize penalties for oversubtraction of faint synthetic features while maintaining smooth residual backgrounds. The final weights used in this study were $[w_1, w_2, w_3, w_4, w_5] = [0.4, 0.1, 0.3, 0.1, 0.1]$.

Analysis of individual metrics (Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:example_1img_metrics}) shows that no single metric is sufficient to guide optimization. Similarly, optimization using only the two dominant metrics ($w_1 = w_3 = 0.5$) tends to under-subtract the background, demonstrating the need for a multi-term objective function.


\subsubsection{Parameter Optimization}

The aggregate loss function is minimized to identify optimal parameter values for each background subtraction method. Because these methods typically involve non-differentiable operations and discrete parameter choices (e.g.\ window size or number of iterations), gradient-based optimization is not well suited. Instead, direct search strategies that rely solely on function evaluations are employed, despite their higher computational cost.

We use a coordinate descent strategy in which parameters are optimized sequentially, with all other parameters held fixed at their current best values. Input parameters are either integer-valued or exhibit no qualitative sensitivity to fractional changes. For each algorithm, predefined initial values and parameter bounds are specified. Across the datasets considered here, optimized parameter values for all methods fall within the range $[0, 300]$, although these bounds may be adjusted for alternative implementations.

Optimization proceeds using a decreasing step-size schedule, with step sizes ranging from 50 to 1 as the loss value improves. Parameters are processed in order of estimated significance. After the main coordinate descent loop, a localized grid search is performed around the best solution to refine parameters jointly.

To improve computational efficiency, images are downsampled prior to background subtraction and upsampled for evaluation. Only the estimated background is downsampled, which is then subtracted from the original-resolution image to preserve diffraction signal fidelity. The impact of downsampling depends on the background subtraction method (Appendix Tables~\ref{tab:downsample_cs}--\ref{tab:downsample_rw}). Downsampling is applied only when it leads to reduced loss values: a factor of 2 is used for G-ILPF, B-ILPF, and WTH; a factor of 4 for RW; and no downsampling for CS, RCH, or AVG (see Section~3.4.1).

Processing of individual images is parallelized using multiprocessing to reduce overall runtime.

\section{Results}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

This section summarizes the performance of background subtraction methods evaluated using the proposed framework. Methods are compared using the aggregate loss function and its constituent metrics, with lower values indicating improved background removal, reduced artifacts, and better preservation of faint diffraction features. We first present a quantitative comparison across datasets, followed by analysis of optimized parameters and representative visual examples.

Table~\ref{tab:method_performance_new} reports the aggregate loss and individual metric values for each background subtraction method, averaged over all evaluated datasets. The number of successfully processed images is shown for each method, reflecting differences in algorithm robustness and convergence behavior. Lower values indicate better performance for all reported metrics.

Overall, Gaussian Iterative Low-Pass Filtering (G-ILPF) achieves the lowest aggregate loss among the evaluated methods. In addition to strong quantitative performance, G-ILPF requires only two tunable parameters, making it a practical and computationally efficient choice. Boxcar Iterative Low-Pass Filtering (B-ILPF), White Top-Hat filtering (WTH), and Circularly Symmetric (CS) background subtraction also perform competitively, with moderate differences in specific metrics. In contrast, the mean-value baseline (AVG) exhibits substantially higher loss across all metrics, confirming the importance of structured background modeling.

The individual metric values reveal trade-offs between methods. For example, low-pass filtering approaches generally achieve low synthetic mean squared error, indicating good preservation of faint injected features, while convex-hull-based methods tend to exhibit larger residual baseline values. No single metric dominates performance across all methods, underscoring the need for a composite evaluation criterion.

Optimized parameter values for each method are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:optimized_params}. The fitted coefficients show considerable variability both across datasets and within individual datasets. This variability reflects differences in background structure arising from experimental conditions, detector characteristics, and sample preparation. The observed spread suggests that further performance gains may be achievable by clustering diffraction patterns based on background characteristics and selecting method parameters accordingly.

Figure~\ref{fig:example_image_loss} illustrates representative examples corresponding to low-loss and high-loss background subtraction outcomes. The low-loss example shows effective removal of diffuse background while preserving diffraction features and maintaining a smooth residual baseline. In contrast, the high-loss example exhibits incomplete background removal, with elevated residual intensity between layer lines, resulting in increased loss values. These examples demonstrate that the quantitative metrics correlate well with visual assessment of background subtraction quality.

The framework also adapts to images with weak diffraction features. Figure~\ref{fig:example_faint} shows a low-intensity diffraction pattern and the corresponding background estimate. Even in this challenging regime, the evaluation framework guides parameter optimization toward solutions that suppress background without eliminating faint structural features.

Taken together, these results demonstrate that the proposed framework provides a consistent and objective basis for comparing background subtraction methods across diverse datasets. The framework not only identifies methods that perform well on average, but also highlights variability in optimal parameter values, pointing toward future extensions that incorporate dataset-specific or image-specific adaptation.

\section{Discussion}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

This section discusses alternative modeling approaches and practical observations that complement the primary results. In particular, we examine parametric background models and equatorial streak modeling, highlighting their advantages, limitations, and potential for future integration with the proposed evaluation framework.


\subsection{Parametric Background Models}

In addition to the non-parametric background subtraction methods evaluated in this work, we explored parametric models for the general background. Scattering from isotropic components in diffraction experiments is expected to produce approximately circular intensity distributions, which are naturally described in polar coordinates. In muscle fiber diffraction data, the general background is observed to be nearly circularly symmetric, with mild elongation along the equatorial direction.

The parametric model that produced the most plausible results combines a power-law decay with a low-order orthogonal polynomial expansion,
\begin{equation}
B(q) = \sum_{j=0}^{3} a_j\, T_j(\tilde q) + A_{\mathrm{p}}\, (q + q_0)^{-p},
\end{equation}
where $T_j$ denotes Chebyshev polynomials and $q$ is the radial coordinate. Model parameters are estimated by least-squares fitting to radial intensity projections. To account for slight equatorial elongation, projections are extracted at $\pm 5^{\circ}$ from the meridian, along with an additional projection representing the average circular background. After fitting, a two-dimensional background image is reconstructed and evaluated using the same metrics applied to non-parametric methods.

Despite producing visually plausible background estimates, the parametric approach underperforms relative to the non-parametric methods considered in this study, primarily due to oversubtraction (Table~\ref{tab:param_performance}). This occurs even when negative residuals are weighted more strongly during least-squares fitting. The reduced flexibility of the parametric model limits its ability to accommodate deviations from the assumed background shape, which can result in artifacts when experimental conditions vary. At the same time, the parametric approach offers advantages, including reduced sensitivity to noise and a lower risk of overfitting. An example of a parametric background fit is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:example_parametric}.

\subsection{Equatorial Streak Modeling}

Muscle fiber diffraction patterns exhibit a pronounced equatorial streak arising from aligned rod-like structures, which presents a particular challenge for background subtraction due to its high intensity and anisotropic shape. Among the non-parametric methods evaluated here, the two-dimensional Radial Convex Hull (RCH) approach is the most effective at suppressing the equatorial streak. However, RCH often introduces artifacts at higher scattering angles, where the angular projection cones span larger regions of the detector. Combining RCH with other background subtraction methods can produce visually plausible results, but transitions between methods may introduce additional artifacts.

Parametric modeling of equatorial streaks has been developed for polymer diffraction data using elliptical coordinates and Pearson VII functions~\cite{MurthyZeroGrubb1997}. Rod-like particles in both polymer and biological samples produce streak-like diffraction features in reciprocal space, suggesting that similar models may be applicable to muscle fiber diffraction.

In this approach, the general background is first removed to isolate the equatorial streak. The streak is then modeled using the parametric form and fitted via least-squares minimization. Because two-dimensional fitting is computationally intensive, the diffraction patterns are downsampled by a factor of two during the fitting stage. After subtraction of the equatorial streak, the previously removed general background can be added back, allowing the optimization procedure described in Section~3.3 to select the most appropriate general background method.

This combined approach yields plausible results across the full diffraction pattern, including the equatorial region. However, accurate fitting of the equatorial streak is sensitive to masking of the beam center and layer lines, which currently requires operator intervention. Representative examples of equatorial background modeling are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:eq_2d_subtracted}.

\subsection{Practical Observations and Limitations}

Several practical observations emerged during this study. Applying guided filtering to smooth diffraction images prior to background estimation improves performance for several non-parametric methods, particularly the white top-hat (WTH) filter, which is sensitive to high-frequency noise. Importantly, smoothing is applied only to estimate the background; the background-subtracted image is computed using the original, unsmoothed data, ensuring that diffraction features are preserved.

More broadly, these observations highlight the trade-off between model flexibility, robustness, and operator intervention. While non-parametric methods combined with the proposed evaluation framework offer strong overall performance and adaptability, parametric approaches provide complementary advantages that may be valuable in more constrained or noise-limited settings. Future work may focus on hybrid strategies that integrate parametric modeling with data-driven evaluation to further improve robustness and automation.

\section{Conclusion}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

This work presents a quantitative, physics-informed framework for the evaluation and optimization of background subtraction methods in muscle fiber diffraction images. By formalizing qualitative expectations of successful background removal and translating them into objective, reproducible metrics, the framework enables systematic comparison of existing algorithms and data-driven selection of method parameters. The introduction of synthetic diffraction features provides a practical mechanism for assessing preservation of faint signal in the absence of ground-truth background, addressing a fundamental challenge in experimental diffraction analysis.

Applied across diverse synchrotron diffraction datasets, the framework identifies background subtraction methods that balance artifact suppression, background smoothness, and signal preservation. In particular, iterative low-pass filtering approaches achieve strong overall performance while remaining computationally practical, requiring only a small number of tunable parameters. The evaluation results demonstrate that no single metric is sufficient in isolation, and that a composite objective function is necessary to capture the competing physical and algorithmic constraints inherent to background subtraction.

The framework is flexible and extensible. While the primary focus of this work is non-parametric background removal, parametric models for the general background and equatorial streak were also explored. Although these parametric approaches did not outperform non-parametric methods within the present evaluation framework, they highlight complementary strengths, including reduced sensitivity to noise and controlled model behavior. For many downstream analyses, such as layer-line characterization, removal of the general background alone is sufficient, while precise equatorial streak modeling may be reserved for applications that require full two-dimensional intensity recovery.

Several limitations should be noted. The optimization procedure is computationally demanding, as background subtraction methods must be evaluated repeatedly to identify suitable parameter values. In practice, limiting the number of function evaluations provides a reasonable trade-off between runtime and solution quality, but may result in suboptimal parameter selection. In addition, variability in optimized parameters across datasets reflects underlying differences in experimental conditions and sample properties, suggesting that further gains may be achieved through dataset-specific or image-specific adaptation.

Future work may focus on relating evaluation metrics and optimized parameters more directly to experimental conditions and physical properties of the sample, with the goal of improving interpretability and automation. Incorporating adaptive or hybrid strategies that combine parametric modeling with data-driven evaluation represents a promising direction. More broadly, the proposed framework establishes a foundation for reproducible, objective background subtraction in fiber diffraction and can be extended to other diffraction and imaging modalities characterized by complex, non-uniform backgrounds.
