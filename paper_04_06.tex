\begin{synopsis}
A quantitative framework is presented for evaluating and optimizing background subtraction methods for synchrotron X-ray fiber diffraction patterns from striated muscle.
\end{synopsis}


%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\begin{abstract}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Accurate background subtraction is a critical preprocessing step in the analysis of X-ray fiber diffraction patterns from striated muscle, where diffuse scattering, instrumental artifacts, and specimen-dependent features complicate the separation of signal and background. Existing background subtraction methods are often applied heuristically, with limited guidance on parameter selection or objective evaluation, leading to variability in downstream quantitative analyses.

In this work, we present a unified framework for the systematic evaluation and optimization of background subtraction methods for muscle fiber diffraction images. The framework combines physically motivated image-quality metrics, synthetic signal validation, and a global optimization strategy to identify method-specific parameter settings that balance background suppression with signal preservation. Multiple commonly used background subtraction approaches are evaluated within a consistent pipeline, enabling direct quantitative comparison across datasets.

We apply the framework to synchrotron X-ray fiber diffraction patterns from striated muscle acquired under varied experimental conditions. Optimized parameter selection substantially improves background removal consistency and reduces artifacts relative to default or ad hoc settings, while preserving key diffraction features such as equatorial reflections and layer lines. The aggregate loss derived from the proposed metrics correlates with expert visual assessment and highlights characteristic failure modes of different subtraction strategies.

This framework provides an objective, reproducible basis for selecting and tuning background subtraction methods in muscle fiber diffraction studies. Although developed for striated muscle diffraction, the approach is based on general signal and background properties and can be extended to other classes of fiber diffraction data. By formalizing evaluation and optimization, it supports more reliable quantitative analysis of synchrotron diffraction experiments.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Reliable background subtraction is essential for quantitative analysis of muscle fiber diffraction patterns, but remains challenging due to strong, non-uniform scattering that can obscure weak diffraction features. In this section we motivate the problem, summarize limitations of current approaches, and outline the contributions of the proposed framework.

\subsection{Motivation}

Scientific imaging techniques used in structural biology and materials science that rely on diffraction analysis often face a common challenge: extracting weak diffraction signals from images dominated by complex, non-uniform backgrounds, which arise from amorphous regions within the sample, lattice disorder, instrumental artifacts, and scattering from the experimental environment. Together, these contributions can obscure subtle diffraction features that are critical for quantitative analysis.

X-ray fiber diffraction provides unique insight into the sarcomere-level molecular structure of muscle and has proven valuable for studying the structural consequences of cardiac and skeletal myopathies. Analysis of muscle diffraction patterns can therefore contribute to understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying diseases ranging from nemaline myopathy to heart failure~\cite{MaIrving2022}. Accurate interpretation of these diffraction patterns requires reliable intensity measurements and effective background subtraction. Muscle diffraction patterns have, in addition to the equatorial and meridional peaks and the continuous layer lines arising from the quasi-helical actin and myosin filaments, amorphous scattering from a large fraction of non-coherently diffracting material. In small-angle X-ray fiber diffraction experiments on muscle fibers, therefore, the signal of interest consists of diffraction peaks of varying intensity superimposed on a strong, spatially non-uniform diffuse background that must be removed prior to analysis~\cite{squire2003ccp13_software}.

Analysis of two-dimensional fiber diffraction patterns from partially crystalline structures such as muscle fibers using standard crystallographic techniques is challenging. In addition to diffuse background scattering, muscle diffraction patterns exhibit continuous bands of intensity along the layer lines and complex meridional reflection patterns arising from multiple co-existing structures within the sample. As a result, fiber diffraction data are commonly analyzed using one-dimensional projections followed by background removal and parametric peak evaluation. Such one-dimensional analyses are effective for sharp reflections along the meridional and equatorial axes, where a large fraction of the signal is concentrated. However, they are less suitable for studying continuous features such as layer lines, where distinguishing diffraction signal from background is inherently difficult. A robust approach to background removal applied directly to two-dimensional diffraction patterns would therefore enable more comprehensive extraction of structural information, including features that are not typically analyzed systematically.

Existing two-dimensional background subtraction methods commonly rely on non-parametric techniques, such as morphological filtering or iterative smoothing. These approaches typically require manual parameter selection and may inadvertently remove weak diffraction signals. Because the intensity of the background often exceeds that of the signal by orders of magnitude, even small errors in background subtraction can significantly affect downstream quantitative analysis. An objective, physics-informed framework for evaluating background subtraction performance would facilitate systematic comparison of existing methods and support the development of improved approaches. More broadly, approaches aimed towards reliable separation of weak signals from complex backgrounds have the potential to enhance data fidelity across a wide range of scientific imaging applications.

\subsection{Problem Statement}

The separation of signal from background is a fundamental problem in quantitative imaging. In current approaches to background removal for muscle fiber diffraction patterns, operator intervention is typically required to visually assess subtraction quality and to manually adjust algorithm parameters. Such manual processing introduces several challenges. First, weak diffraction signals may be lost due to the large intensity contrast between background and peaks, combined with the presence of noise that can mask low-intensity features. Second, reliance on visual assessment introduces subjectivity, leading to solutions that may be plausible but not optimal. Finally, reproducibility depends on careful documentation and reuse of user-selected parameters.

We pose the question of whether we can find the optimal solution to the problem of background subtraction in the absence of ground truth. We claim that the absence of a standardized, quantitative evaluation framework limits the ability to objectively compare background subtraction algorithms and impedes systematic parameter optimization. This lack of objective assessment hinders reproducibility and slows methodological progress.

\subsection{Scope}

This work presents a unified framework for improving the analysis of weak diffraction signals in the presence of complex, non-uniform backgrounds. The framework replaces subjective visual assessment with quantitative, physics-informed evaluation criteria and combines these metrics with synthetic-signal validation and automated parameter optimization. The main contributions are as follows:

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{Quantitative assessment of background subtraction quality given lack of ground truth}. A set of reproducible quantitative metrics is derived from physical properties of the signal generation process. These measures provide a reproducible standard for assessing background subtraction quality and enable unsupervised comparison of algorithms and parameter sets.

\item \textbf{Sampling framework for measuring fine detail preservation}. Injection of faint synthetic diffraction features to penalize signal loss during background subtraction, ensuring that weak but physically meaningful signals are preserved.

\item \textbf{Optimization procedure for background subtraction on muscle fiber diffraction patterns}. A unified loss and optimization pipeline for automated method-specific parameter selection, which enables systematic comparison of existing background subtraction methods. The procedure has been incorporated in MuscleX, a software package for muscle fiber diffraction analysis, and is available for use.

\end{enumerate}

Although developed in the context of muscle fiber diffraction, the framework is based on general signal and background properties and is therefore applicable to other imaging modalities characterized by complex, non-uniform backgrounds.

The framework addresses the need for improved background subtraction using existing methods while also providing a tool for ongoing methodological development in quantitative image analysis. \section{Related Work}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Background removal strategies in diffraction imaging depend strongly on both the dimensionality of the data and the structural characteristics of the sample. In muscle fiber diffraction, background subtraction is commonly performed either on one-dimensional intensity profiles extracted from the data or directly on two-dimensional diffraction patterns. This section reviews representative approaches in both categories and highlights their limitations in the context of biological fiber diffraction.

\subsection{One-dimensional background subtraction}

Background removal in diffraction analysis is traditionally performed on one-dimensional intensity profiles extracted along directions of interest, most commonly the equator and meridian. In standard practice, diffraction peaks are analyzed individually, and background intensity is estimated by averaging signal in regions adjacent to each peak, which is then subtracted from the integrated peak intensity~\cite{NederKorsunskiy2018}. This approach is simple and effective for isolated reflections, but depends on careful selection of background regions and does not account for spatial variation in the background.

Another commonly used technique applies the convex hull algorithm~\cite{jarvis1973convex_hull} to one-dimensional intensity profiles to separate diffraction peaks from a smoothly varying background~\cite{lee2019musclex}. While robust to large-scale intensity variations, the convex hull method does not explicitly model background noise, and this can introduce systematic errors in quantitative measurements. For crystalline powder diffraction patterns, a variety of methods estimate a slowly varying smooth curve underneath the sharp peaks~\cite{bruckner2000background, PolarBackground2024, ning2014beads, baek2015baseline, prescher2015dioptas}; however, muscle diffraction peaks are less well defined and are more difficult to isolate from the background.

Parametric background models are also widely used in one-dimensional analyses. Low-order polynomials, Chebyshev polynomials, or spline functions are commonly employed to represent slowly varying backgrounds, while exponential components may be introduced to model steeper background variations~\cite{rajkumar2007fibrefix}. Although these approaches are effective for sharp reflections along the equator and meridian, they inherently discard spatial information contained in the two-dimensional shapes of diffraction features. As a result, one-dimensional methods provide limited access to continuous diffraction features such as layer lines, which contain important structural information.

\subsection{Two-dimensional background subtraction}

Before applying any background subtraction technique, a sample-free measurement is typically subtracted to remove instrumental artifacts and air scattering. Two-dimensional background subtraction methods operate directly on diffraction patterns and aim to preserve spatial information that is lost in one-dimensional projections.

Some two-dimensional approaches rely on explicit assumptions about the geometry or smoothness of the background. Radial symmetry methods assume that the background intensity is symmetric about the beam center and estimate it by averaging pixel intensities over concentric rings~\cite{rajkumar2007fibrefix}. This method is practical for crystalline powder diffraction where the background is isotropic and can often contain rings or halos~\cite{ashiotis2015pyfai}. While computationally efficient, this assumption breaks down in the presence of anisotropic background features, such as the equatorial streak characteristic of muscle fiber diffraction. Partition-based methods divide the diffraction pattern into local regions, estimate the background within each region, and interpolate between regions to form a continuous background image. Examples include the roving window method~\cite{HeEtAl2007} and the radial convex hull approach, which applies one-dimensional convex hull subtraction to angular projections of the pattern~\cite{lee2019musclex}. Although these methods can adapt to spatially varying backgrounds, their performance depends sensitively on partition size and angular resolution and may introduce artifacts in regions with steep background gradients. In particular, the radial convex hull method tends to perform poorly at high scattering angles, where angular sectors span large regions of the detector.

Other methods treat background subtraction as an image processing problem, exploiting differences in spatial scale or frequency content between background and diffraction features. Iterative low-pass filtering assumes that background variations occur at lower spatial frequencies than diffraction peaks and estimates the background through repeated smoothing~\cite{IvanovaMakowski1998}. Its effectiveness depends strongly on the chosen filter scale and the number of iterations, and weak or low-contrast diffraction features may be partially removed. Morphological filtering techniques, such as white top-hat operations~\cite{GonzalezWoodsDIP4e}, estimate the background using a structuring element and subtract it from the original image. Performance depends on the size of the structuring element relative to feature size, and inappropriate choices may suppress extended or low-intensity diffraction signals. Frequency-domain approaches based on Fourier or wavelet transforms separate image components by spatial frequency. These techniques are effective for crystalline diffraction patterns with sharp features~\cite{PolarBackground2024, WaveletBiomedOptExpress2021, renedecotret2017baseline}, but are less suitable for fiber diffraction, where signal and background overlap in frequency space. Kernel-based rolling-ball methods~\cite{Sternberg1983} similarly perform well for crystalline samples, but are generally less effective for fiber diffraction data.

More recently, data-driven approaches have been explored. Supervised deep learning methods can be trained to perform various image processing tasks, including background subtraction, when sufficient labeled data are available~\cite{archana2024dl_image_processing_review}. However, with fiber diffraction patterns, the lack of ground-truth background and the difficulty of generating representative training datasets limit the practical applicability of such approaches. A neural network has been used to learn muscle fiber diffraction backgrounds based on non-parametric method estimates~\cite{ArangurenCarmona2020}. The model is trained on non-ground truth labels, and it is also not constrained and may alter the pattern, hindering further analysis. Deep learning has also been widely used for function approximation~\cite{devore2021nn_approximation}; however, there is no agreed upon functional form of the background in muscle fiber diffraction patterns, which varies across samples and experimental conditions. Traditionally in machine learning, background subtraction refers to foreground separation which involves precise edge detection~\cite{bouwmans2019dnn_background_subtraction}. The diffraction features that are superimposed on top of the background may be considered the foreground; however, their edges are not well-defined. Background removal in other contexts can also refer to noise removal~\cite{jiang2025efficient_denoising_survey}; in fiber diffraction, noise should be preserved while the high intensity smooth background component should be removed.  In patterns from crystalline systems, where background removal is less of a challenge compared to fiber diffraction, deep learning generally has been used for a variety of tasks, including pattern classification~\cite{aguiar2020crystallographic_prediction, ziletti2018dl_crystal_classification}, labelling~\cite{chavez2025mlexchange_labeling}, interpretation of patterns~\cite{szymanski2021probabilistic_multiphase_xrd} and inpainting of missing data~\cite{chavez2022dl_inpainting_xray,roberts2024dlsia}.

Across all two-dimensional methods, the selection of algorithms and parameters typically relies on qualitative visual assessment by domain experts. This reliance introduces subjectivity and increases the risk of suppressing subtle diffraction features. The absence of standardized, quantitative evaluation criteria motivates the development of objective measures for comparing background subtraction methods and guiding systematic parameter optimization, as proposed in this work.

\section{Approach}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

This section describes the methodological framework used to evaluate and optimize background subtraction methods for muscle fiber diffraction patterns. We first define qualitative objectives and quantitative metrics that capture physically meaningful properties of successful background removal. To enable objective validation in the absence of ground truth, synthetic diffraction features and region-specific masking are introduced. These metrics are then combined into an aggregate loss function that guides parameter optimization for each background subtraction method. Finally, the experimental design, including algorithm variants and dataset selection, is described.


\subsection{Background}



\subsection{Evaluation Framework}

The evaluation framework is designed to provide objective, physically meaningful criteria for assessing the quality of background subtraction in muscle fiber diffraction images. Because ground-truth background is unavailable for experimental data, the framework combines qualitative expectations derived from physical considerations with quantitative metrics that enable reproducible comparison across methods and parameter settings.

%\subsubsection{Qualitative Objectives}

An ideal background-subtracted diffraction image should satisfy several qualitative criteria. True diffraction features should be preserved with minimal distortion and remain clearly distinguishable from the background. The subtraction process should not introduce non-physical artifacts, such as extended negative regions or patterns resembling diffraction features (e.g. layer lines or reflection peaks), which would indicate over-subtraction. Since intensity values are non-negative by definition, negative excursions should be minimized. In regions free of diffraction signal, the residual background should be smooth, near zero, and free of structured intensity variations. Finally, for images acquired under similar experimental conditions, background subtraction results and optimal parameters should be consistent across frames. These qualitative objectives motivate the quantitative metrics used below.

\subsubsection{Quantitative Metrics}

In order to capture deviations from the described objectives in a reproducible way, quantitative metrics are defined. Several metrics operate directly on the background-subtracted image to assess artifact introduction, background smoothness, and residual baseline behavior.

Oversubtraction is assessed by measuring the fraction of negative-valued pixels that belong to spatially connected clusters larger than a specified minimum size (e.g.\ nine pixels). Random noise typically produces isolated negative pixels, whereas systematic oversubtraction produces spatially connected regions. This metric, denoted $f_{\mathrm{neg,con}}$, is ideally zero.

Residual background level is quantified using the fraction of pixels whose intensity lies within a specified threshold of zero, denoted $f_{B}$. This metric reflects how effectively structured background has been removed while preserving natural noise. The threshold is estimated from the average intensity near the edge of the diffraction pattern, where background scattering is expected to be minimal.

Background smoothness is quantified by measuring the average absolute intensity difference between adjacent rows in masked background regions. This smoothness metric, denoted $\mathrm{S}_{\mathrm{BG}}$, captures the presence of structured or oscillatory residual background. It is defined as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{S}_{\mathrm{BG}} =
\frac{1}{N} \sum_i \left| I(y_i + 1) - I(y_i) \right|,
\end{equation}
where the summation is performed over rows within masked background regions to exclude known diffraction features. Smooth residual backgrounds produce low values of this metric.

\subsubsection{Validation Using Synthetic Structures}

Because experimental diffraction images do not provide ground-truth background, synthetic diffraction features are introduced to evaluate preservation of faint signals after background subtraction (see Section~3.2.2). These validation metrics focus on signal loss and oversubtraction behavior.

Preservation of synthetic signal is quantified using the mean squared error between the background-subtracted image and a reference image containing only the injected synthetic features, denoted $\mathrm{MSE}_{\mathrm{syn}}$. Ideally, this value is zero, indicating perfect preservation of synthetic signal.
Although synthetic features are placed in regions known not to contain real diffraction features, partial overlap between synthetic and real structures may increase this metric.

Oversubtraction of synthetic features is further assessed by measuring the fraction of negative-valued pixels remaining after synthetic signal subtraction, denoted $f_{\mathrm{neg,syn}}$. This metric emphasizes non-physical negative excursions introduced by background removal and is ideally zero.

\subsection{Sampling Framework for Assessing Detail Preservation}

This subsection describes synthetic signal design used to enable objective evaluation of background subtraction performance in the absence of ground-truth background. Region-specific masking isolates background-dominated areas, while synthetic diffraction features provide controlled test signals for assessing preservation of faint structure.

\subsubsection{Synthetic Signal Validation Design}

To address the central limitation in the lack of ground-truth, we inject synthetic diffraction features resembling real signal into the images prior to background subtraction as summarized in Algorithm~\ref{alg:synthetic_signal}. Preservation of these features after subtraction provides a controlled measure of signal loss and oversubtraction.
% Algorithm~\ref{alg:synthetic_signal} outlines the procedure for generating synthetic features, which are designed to mimic real diffraction features while being placed in regions that do not contain known real signal.


\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\centering
\begin{minipage}{0.96\columnwidth}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]

\Require Image $I$, prior peaks $(i'_{0,1}, m'_1)$, controls $(f, \alpha, z_x, z_y)$
\Ensure Synthetic image $I^{syn}$, artificial-only image $I^{art}$, mask $M$

\State Detect equator peak $i_{0,1} \leftarrow \textsc{FindEquatorPeak}(I)$
\If{$|i_{0,1} - i'_{0,1}| > \delta$}
    \State $i_{0,1} \leftarrow i'_{0,1}$
\EndIf

\State $\sigma_x \leftarrow i_{0,1} / z_x$
\State $\sigma_y \leftarrow m'_1 / z_y$
\State $A \leftarrow \alpha \cdot \text{Equator}(i_{0,1}) \cdot i_{0,1}$

\State $K \leftarrow \textsc{GaussianKernel}(A, \sigma_x, \sigma_y)$
\State $G \leftarrow \textsc{SamplingGrid}(f, i_{0,1}, m'_1)$

\State Initialize $I^{art} \leftarrow 0$, $M \leftarrow 0$

\ForAll{$(x,y) \in G$}
    \State $I^{art} \leftarrow I^{art} + \textsc{PasteKernel}(K, x, y)$
    \State Mark support of $K$ at $(x,y)$ in $M$
\EndFor

\State $I^{art} \gets \textsc{IntensityDecay}(I^{art})$

\State $I^{syn} \leftarrow I + I^{art}$

\State \Return $(I^{syn}, I^{art}, M)$

\end{algorithmic}
\end{minipage}
\caption{Procedure for synthetic-feature generation and mask construction used for validation in the absence of ground-truth background.}
\label{alg:synthetic_signal}
\end{algorithm}


Synthetic features are modeled using two-dimensional Gaussian functions elongated along the horizontal axis, reflecting the typical shape of diffraction features such as layer lines. The synthetic feature is defined as
\begin{equation}
G(x, y) = A \exp\!\left(
-\frac{(x-x_0)^2}{2\sigma_x^2}
-\frac{(y-y_0)^2}{2\sigma_y^2}
\right),
\end{equation}
where $A$ is the amplitude, $(x_0, y_0)$ is the feature center, and $\sigma_x$ and $\sigma_y$ are the standard deviations along the horizontal and vertical axes, respectively. This formulation corresponds to an axis-aligned Gaussian without rotational components.

The shape parameters are selected automatically for each image to match the scale of real diffraction features. Based on observed correlations in experimental data, $\sigma_x$ and $\sigma_y$ are derived from the average FWHM of prominent reflections, specifically the $I_{1,0}$ equatorial peak and the M1 and M3 meridional reflections. Specifically,
\begin{equation}
\sigma_x = \frac{I_{1,0}}{5} \times \frac{1}{2\sqrt{2\ln2}}, 
\qquad
\sigma_y = \frac{\mathrm{M1}}{20} \times \frac{1}{2\sqrt{2\ln2}}.
\end{equation}

The radial decay of synthetic feature intensity follows a $1/r$ model, where $r$ is the distance from the pattern center. This choice reflects the scattering geometry of X-ray diffraction and approximates the Lorentz correction, which for equatorial and meridional reflections can be expressed as $L \propto 1/\sin 2\theta$~\cite{Buerger1940}. At small scattering angles, $\theta$ is approximately proportional to $r$, making a $1/r$ decay a reasonable approximation~\cite{Denny1995}.

The maximum amplitude of synthetic features is chosen to align, at each radius, with the average intensity of real diffraction features. For each image, the amplitude is scaled relative to the strongest observed reflection, the $I_{1,0}$ equatorial peak. Setting the maximum synthetic feature amplitude to $A_{\mathrm{max}} = 1\%\, I_{1,0}$ ensures that injected features remain faint while spanning a range of scattering angles.

Synthetic features are placed at regular intervals across the image to ensure broad spatial coverage. The sampling frequency $(f_x, f_y)$ controls placement density and is chosen to avoid overlap with known diffraction features. Layer line positions are explicitly accounted for to ensure that synthetic features probe background-dominated regions.

\subsubsection{Region-Specific Masking}

Accurate calculation of evaluation metrics requires separating regions containing diffraction signal from those dominated by background or artifacts. To achieve this, multiple complementary masks are applied.

% Regions containing non-representative or saturated signal are excluded using a main signal mask. In fiber diffraction images, this mask removes the high-intensity central region associated with the direct beam and detector edges. The mask is defined as an annulus with inner radius $r_{\mathrm{min}}$ and outer radius $r_{\mathrm{max}}$.

% The strong equatorial background streak characteristic of muscle fiber diffraction is isolated using a dedicated equator background mask. Because the equatorial streak is typically more intense than the surrounding diffuse background, it is treated separately. This mask is implemented as a rectangular region aligned with the equator.

% Regions containing synthetic diffraction features are identified using a synthetic feature mask. Around each injected synthetic structure, a rectangular area extending $2\sigma$ in each direction is masked, where $\sigma$ denotes the standard deviation of the synthetic feature.

% To avoid contamination from real diffraction signal, regions where genuine diffraction features are expected are excluded using a real feature mask. Layer line regions are masked using horizontal bands centered at known layer line positions. The width of each band is determined by estimating the full width at half maximum (FWHM) of the corresponding layer line using perpendicular intensity projections.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textit{Main signal mask:} an annulus excluding beam center and detector edges ($r_{\mathrm{min}}$, $r_{\mathrm{max}}$). This mask excludes regions containing non-representative or saturated signal.
\item \textit{Equator mask:} a rectangle aligned with the equator. Since the equatorial streak is typically more intense than the surrounding diffuse background, it is treated separately.
\item \textit{Synthetic mask:} rectangles of extent $2\sigma$ around each injected feature.
\item \textit{Real-feature mask:} bands at layer-line positions with widths set from estimated FWHM via perpendicular projections. This mask is used to avoid contamination from real diffraction signal.
\end{itemize}

Together, these masks restrict metric evaluation to regions dominated by background, ensuring that evaluation reflects background subtraction quality rather than signal content.





\subsection{Optimization Procedure}

This subsection describes how the evaluation metrics are combined into a single objective function, which is minimized to tune each method's parameters.

\subsubsection{Aggregate Loss Function}

Background subtraction quality is quantified using a weighted aggregate loss function that combines the normalized metrics defined in the evaluation framework (Table~\ref{tab:loss_terms}). The objective function is constructed to balance physically meaningful constraints, artifact suppression, and preservation of faint diffraction signal. It is defined as
\begin{equation}
%\begin{aligned}
L =\;
\lambda_1\, S_{\mathrm{BG}}
+ \lambda_2\, f_{\mathrm{neg,con}}
+ \lambda_3\, f_{\mathrm{neg,syn}}
+ \lambda_4\, \mathrm{MSE}_{\mathrm{syn}}
+ \lambda_5\, \bigl(1 - f_{B}\bigr),
%\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where each metric is normalized by its average value across datasets prior to aggregation, ensuring comparable scale during optimization.

The weights $\lambda_i$ encode the relative importance of different physical and algorithmic considerations and may be adjusted to reflect user priorities. We compared equal weighting ($1\!:\!1\!:\!1\!:\!1\!:\!1$) to a domain-informed scheme ($3\!:\!3\!:\!2\!:\!2\!:\!1$) that emphasizes smoothness and oversubtraction penalties over the baseline term. In the latter case, higher weights are assigned to metrics enforcing direct physical constraints, such as background smoothness and suppression of connected negative regions. 

Although the optimization is driven by quantitative metrics, visual inspection remains necessary to identify artifacts that are difficult to capture numerically, such as faint negative excursions or distortion of layer lines. Based on visual comparison, domain-informed weighting consistently produced better results than equal weighting. Further adjustment was applied to emphasize penalties for oversubtraction of faint synthetic features while maintaining smooth residual backgrounds. The final weights used in this study were $[\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3, \lambda_4, \lambda_5] = [0.4, 0.1, 0.3, 0.1, 0.1]$.

Analysis of individual metrics (Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:example_1img_metrics}) shows that no single metric is sufficient to guide optimization. Similarly, optimization using only the two dominant metrics ($\lambda_1 = \lambda_3 = 0.5$) tends to under-subtract the background, demonstrating the need for a multi-term objective function.


\subsubsection{Parameter Optimization}

The aggregate loss function is minimized to identify optimal parameter values for each background subtraction method. Because these methods typically involve non-differentiable operations and discrete parameter choices (e.g.\ window size or number of smoothing cycles), gradient-based optimization is not well suited. Instead, direct search strategies that rely solely on function evaluations are employed, despite their higher computational cost.

To minimize $L$ we use a coordinate-descent strategy in which parameters are optimized sequentially, in the order of their estimated significance, with all other parameters held fixed at their current best values. Input parameters are either integer-valued or exhibit no qualitative sensitivity to fractional changes. For each algorithm, predefined initial values and parameter bounds are specified. Across the datasets considered here, optimized parameter values for all methods fall within the range $[0, 300]$, although these bounds may be adjusted for alternative implementations.

Optimization proceeds using a decreasing multi-resolution step-size schedule, with step sizes ranging from 50 to 1. As the loss value improves, the step size is restarted from fine to coarese to avoid getting stuck in local minima. After the main coordinate descent loop, a localized grid search is performed around the best solution to jointly refine parameters.

Images are processed in parallel using multiprocessing to reduce overall runtime. Additionally, for further computational efficiency, images are downsampled prior to background estimation and evaluated at full resolution. Only the estimated background is downsampled; it is then subtracted from the original-resolution image to preserve diffraction signal fidelity. The impact of downsampling depends on the background subtraction method (Appendix Tables~\ref{tab:downsample_cs}--\ref{tab:downsample_rw}). Downsampling is applied only when it leads to reduced loss values: a factor of 2 is used for G-ILPF, B-ILPF, and WTH; a factor of 4 for RW; and no downsampling for CS, RCH, or AVG (see Section~3.4.1).

\subsection{Equatorial Streak Background Subtraction}

Muscle fiber diffraction patterns exhibit a pronounced equatorial streak arising from aligned rod-like structures. This feature presents a particular challenge for background subtraction due to its high intensity and anisotropic shape. 

Among the non-parametric methods, the radial convex hull (RCH) approach is the most effective at suppressing the equatorial streak. However, RCH often introduces artifacts at higher scattering angles, where angular projection cones span larger regions of the detector. Combining RCH with other background subtraction methods can produce visually plausible results, but transitions between methods may introduce additional artifacts.

This section describes an approach to address the challenge of removing equatorial streak using parametric modeling. First, the general semi-isotropic background is removed using a parametric model, ensuring that it doesn't affect further equatorial fitting. Next, the equatorial streak is modelled using a two-dimensional parametric model and subtracted from the image. The general background removed in the first pass is added back to apply the optimization framework described above to remove the semi-isotropic background using best-performing method. The first pass is necessary to ensure that the general background doesn't affect equatorial fitting while preserving the streak due to the strict parametric constraints. The second pass allows the optimization framework to select the best method for removing the remaining background.


\paragraph{Parametric Background Modelling.}

Prior to applying the equatorial streak model, the general background is removed to isolate the equatorial streak. We explored several parametric models for the general background as an alternative to the non-parametric approaches. Scattering from isotropic components in diffraction experiments is expected to produce approximately circular intensity distributions, which are naturally described in polar coordinates. In muscle fiber diffraction data, the general background is observed to be nearly circularly symmetric, with mild elongation along the equatorial direction.

The parametric model that produced the most plausible results combines a power-law decay with a low-order orthogonal polynomial expansion,
\begin{equation}
B(q) = \sum_{j=0}^{3} a_j\, T_j(\tilde q) + A_{\mathrm{p}}\, (q + q_0)^{-p},
\end{equation}
where $T_j$ denotes Chebyshev polynomials and $q$ is the radial coordinate. Model parameters are estimated by least-squares fitting to radial intensity projections. To account for slight equatorial elongation, projections are extracted at $\pm 5^{\circ}$ from the meridian, along with an additional projection representing the average circular background. The fitted background is then subtracted from the original image, resulting in a background-subtracted image that retains the equatorial streak while removing the general background. This step is crucial to ensure that the subsequent parametric modeling of the equatorial streak is not confounded by the presence of the general background, which could bias parameter estimation and lead to suboptimal subtraction results.

\paragraph{Equatorial Streak Modeling.}

Parametric modeling of equatorial streaks has previously been developed for polymer diffraction data using elliptical coordinates and Pearson VII functions~\cite{MurthyZeroGrubb1997}. Rod-like particles in both polymer and biological samples produce streak-like diffraction features in reciprocal space, suggesting that similar models may be applicable to muscle fiber diffraction.

The general background is first removed to isolate the equatorial streak using the parametric model described in Section~4.1. The streak is then modeled using the parametric form used for polymer diffraction data in elliptical coordinates~\cite{MurthyZeroGrubb1997} and fitted via least-squares minimization. Because two-dimensional fitting is computationally intensive, the diffraction patterns are downsampled by a factor of two during the fitting stage. After subtraction of the equatorial streak, the previously removed general background is added back, allowing the optimization procedure described in Section~3.3 to select the most appropriate general background method.

\subsection{Summary of Observations}

Taken together, these results demonstrate that the proposed optimization framework provides a consistent and objective basis for comparing background subtraction methods across diverse datasets. The framework identifies methods that perform well on average while also revealing substantial variability in optimal parameter values. This variability motivates future work on dataset-specific or image-specific parameter selection strategies. Importantly, the framework is intended to guide parameter selection and method adaptation, rather than to prescribe a single fixed algorithm or parameter set.

Combined together with the equatorial streak fitting, the framework allows to perform background subtraction on the whole pattern, which is a significant improvement over existing approaches that typically work on specific regions. Such background modeling allows to separate different background components and enables more accurate analysis of diffraction features across the entire pattern.

\subsubsection{Practical Observations and Limitations}

Several practical observations emerged during this study. Applying guided filtering to smooth diffraction images prior to background estimation improves performance for several non-parametric methods, particularly the white top-hat (WTH) filter, which is sensitive to high-frequency noise. Downsampling the patterns has a similar effect, reducing computational load while smoothing the pattern and thus improving performance. Importantly, smoothing is applied only to estimate the background; the background-subtracted image is computed using the original, unsmoothed data, ensuring that diffraction features are preserved. 

More broadly, the experiments highlight a trade-off between model flexibility, robustness, and operator intervention. Non-parametric methods combined with the proposed evaluation framework offer strong overall performance and adaptability across datasets. In contrast, parametric approaches provide more compact representations and improved noise robustness but are less tolerant of deviations from assumed background structure. The combination of parametric modeling for the equatorial streak with non-parametric methods for the general background shows promise, but further development is needed to achieve robust performance across diverse datasets.

Overall, these observations reinforce the central finding of this study: background subtraction performance depends strongly on image-specific characteristics. This variability suggests that future improvements will likely come from adaptive or hybrid strategies that combine parametric modeling, non-parametric methods, and automated evaluation to select appropriate models and parameters for each diffraction pattern.

\section{Conclusion} 
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

We presented a quantitative, physics-informed framework for the evaluation and optimization of background subtraction methods in muscle fiber diffraction images. By translating qualitative expectations of successful background removal into objective, reproducible metrics, the framework enables systematic comparison of algorithms and data-driven selection of method parameters. The introduction of synthetic diffraction features provides a practical mechanism for assessing preservation of faint signal in the absence of ground-truth background, addressing a fundamental challenge in experimental diffraction analysis.

Across diverse synchrotron datasets, the framework consistently identified background subtraction strategies that balance background smoothness and signal preservation. The best-performing methods differed across datasets, reflecting differences in background structure due to experimental conditions, detectors, and sample preparation and highlighting the importance of dataset-specific optimization. The results also show that no single metric is sufficient in isolation; instead, a composite objective function is required to capture the competing physical and algorithmic constraints inherent to background subtraction.

While the primary focus of this work was on the optimization framework tested with non-parametric background removal methods, exploratory experiments with parametric models for the general background and equatorial streak highlighted complementary strengths. The results suggest that hybrid approaches combining parametric modeling of the equatorial streak with non-parametric methods for general background offer further improvements and allow to perform background subtraction on the whole pattern.
