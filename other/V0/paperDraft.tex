\section{Introduction}
\subsection{Motivation}

Scientific imaging techniques across fields such as structural biology and materials science that employ diffraction analysis
often face a common challenge:
extracting weak diffraction signals from images dominated by complex, non-uniform backgrounds.
The backgrounds may arise from multiple sources, including amorphous regions in the sample, lattice disorder, instrumental artifacts, and scattering from
the surroundings that can collectively obscure the subtle features necessary for quantitative analysis.

X-ray fiber diffraction from muscle fibers can provide unique
insights into the sarcomere-level molecular structure of muscle and be a valuable tool to study structural consequences of
cardiac and skeletal myopathies in muscle. Analysis of muscle diffraction patterns
can be valuable to study the molecular mechanisms of various muscle diseases from nemaline myopathy 
to heart failure ~\cite{MaIrving2022}. The interpretation of X-ray diffraction patterns
requires accurate measurements and correct background removal; the signal of interest consists
of diffraction peaks with different intensities
superimposed on a strong, non-uniform,
diffuse background that needs to be removed prior to analysis ~\cite{ResearchPlanMusico}.
The background in muscle diffraction patterns consists of a general diffuse
background from unstructured material in the sample as well as a strong equatorial streak,
arising from aligned cylindrical objects present in muscle fiber, namely the actin-containing thin filaments and the myosin-containing thick filaments.
The task of background removal is not unique to muscle fiber diffraction, but it is particularly challenging 
because of the overlapping signals from multiple co-existing structures, the large amount of non-coherently diffracting material in the samples, leading
to strong amorphous scattering and the presence of a strong equatorial streak from the rod-shaped myofilaments.

Analysis of two-dimensional fiber diffraction patterns
from partially crystalline structures such as muscle fibers as a whole using
standard crystallographic techniques is challenging, not only due to the diffuse background but also due to the presence of continuous bands of intensity in the layer
lines and the complicated pattern of meridional reflections due to multiple co-existing structures within a muscle fiber sample. 
Consequently, analysis of fiber diffraction data is normally performed using one-dimensional projections, following background removal and parametric peak evaluation.
Such 1D analyses are useful for the analysis of sharp reflections on the meridional and the equatorial axes, where a significant part of the data is located but may be problematic
for studying continuous data such as on the layer lines because of the difficulty in distinguishing the layer line diffraction from the continuous background.
Even with sharp diffraction spots, valuable information regarding degrees of lattice ordering may be contained in the two-dimensional shapes of the reflections,
which is lost with 1D projections.
However, having a robust way of removing background directly in 2D patterns would facilitate extracting
the information present in the structural shape of the diffraction features, which are generally not analyzed systematically.
 

Existing methods for 2D background subtraction use non-parametric techniques,
such as morphological filters or iterative smoothing.
These methods require manual parameter choices and may remove weak signals.
Incorrect background removal affects data analysis significantly due
to the high intensity difference between the background and the signal.
Developing an objective, physics-informed background removal evaluation would assist
in the comparison of existing algorithms and potentially help in the development of new ones.
Addressing the problem of reliable separation of weak diffraction signals
from complex backgrounds could more generally enhance data fidelity
across multiple imaging domains by establishing a foundation for systematic analysis.

\subsection{Problem Statement}
The separation of signal patterns from background is a fundamental problem in quantitative imaging.
In existing approaches for background removal in fiber diffraction patterns from muscle samples, operator intervention is needed to (1)
visually assess the quality of the background-subtracted image and
(2) manually adjust the parameters. 
Manual processing introduces the potential challenge of the loss of weak signals; there is a high intensity difference between background and peaks
as well as the interference of noise, which may mask low-intensity information.
Another challenge is that manual processing introduces subjectivity due to visual qualitative assessment that leads to plausible,
but potentially suboptimal solutions. Reproducibility of results requires saving user-adjusted parameters.
The lack of a standardized evaluation approach impedes parameter optimization for existing algorithms based on more objective quantitative measurements.

\subsection{Scope}

This work describes a framework for enhancing the analysis of weak diffraction signals in the presence of complex, non-uniform backgrounds, 
which is a common challenge across multiple scientific imaging domains. 
% Our contributions establish a method for a more objective, reproducible, and high-quality signal extraction:
\begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{System of Objective Evaluation Measures}. We derive a set of quantitative measures,
        based on physical properties of the signal generation process, which in this case is fiber diffraction from muscle.
        These metrics replace operator-dependent visual assessment with a reproducible standard,
        addressing subjectivity.
        Quantitative measures allow unsupervised
        comparison of background removal algorithms and search for the optimal parameters. 
        \item \textbf{Faint Detail Preservation}. The framework introduces faint synthetic signals and a penalty for the loss
        of synthetic data. This ensures that the subtracted image preserves weak diffraction features.
        \item \textbf{Benchmarking Foundation}. The evaluation framework provides a baseline for the comparison
        of existing and developing background subtraction algorithms, allowing measurement of the significance of improved methods. 
        \item \textbf{Generalizability}. While grounded in the context of muscle fiber diffraction, the framework's
        core principle of using metrics derived from signal physics
        is designed to be adaptable to other scientific imaging domains characterized by complex non-uniform backgrounds. 
\end{enumerate}

This framework solves the immediate problem of improving background subtraction using existing methods as well as provides
a tool for ongoing methodological advancement in background subtraction and subsequent quantitative image analysis.

\section{Related Work}

Existing methods for background removal depend on the dimensionality of the data
and the nature of the sample. Below is an overview of 1D profile techniques and
2D background removal methods.

\subsection{1D Profile Peak Analysis}

Normally, when analyzing the integrated intensity of diffraction peaks, 
specific regions are analyzed separately, and background is removed by taking the weighted average of the regions adjacent
to the diffraction peak and subtracting from the main integrated intensity \cite{NederKorsunskiy2018}.
Another approach is to apply the convex hull algorithm to the 1D intensity profile to isolate the diffraction peaks from the underlying smoothly varying background.
It is a robust approach but does not adequately fit the noise on the background, which can lead to systematic errors.
Alternatively, parametric models may be used to fit the background in 1D projections.
Low-order polynomials, Chebyshev polynomials or splines
are common choices for fitting slowly varying backgrounds. For steeper backgrounds,
exponential components may be added \cite{rajkumar2007fibrefix}.

\subsection{2D Pattern Background Removal Methods}

Direct 2D background removal techniques can be broadly categorized by the underlying algorithmic strategy.
Prior to background removal, a sample-free measurement should be subtracted from the pattern,
thus, removing instrumental artifacts and air scattering.

    \subsubsection{Assumption-Based Methods}
        Such techniques assume the pattern's symmetry or background parametric model.

        \textbf{Radial Symmetry Model.} \emph{Circularly Symmetric} method assumes the background is radially symmetric from the center of the pattern
        and averages pixels in concentric rings. It is computationally simple, however, unable to handle non-radially symmetric backgrounds,
        for example, the equatorial streak typical of muscle fiber diffraction \cite{rajkumar2007fibrefix}.
        
        \textbf{Partitioned Interpolation.} These methods divide the pattern, process local segments and interpolate to form the resulting image. 
        The \emph{Roving Window} method, attributed to Paul Langan and incorporated in the CCP13 suite \cite{HeEtAl2007},
        averages windows of specified size across the image. 
        The \emph{Radial Convex Hull} method projects the 2D pattern along 1-degree angular cones and
        applies a 1D convex hull subtraction to each projection \cite{MuscleXQuadrantFolding}. 
        Although these methods can adapt to varying backgrounds,
        they are sensitive to partition size which may introduce artifacts
        in high-gradient regions of the background. The radial convex hull algorithm tends not to perform
        well at high angles due to the increased absolute size of the cone in comparison to low angles.

        \textbf{Parametric Surface Fitting.} Background may be fitted to 2D functions, e.g.
        an even-ordered polynomial function or a finite double Fourier-Bessel series 
        \cite{LorenzHolmes1993, MillaneArnott1985, Makowski1978}.
        These functions cannot fit well complex backgrounds typical
        of fiber diffraction patterns from biological samples, especially at small angles close to the
        backstop near the beam center.

    \subsubsection{Filter and Transform-Based Methods}
        These approaches perform background removal as an image processing task,
        leveraging such properties as local contrast or spatial frequency.

        \textbf{Multi-Scale Filtering.}
        The iterative low-pass filtering technique uses the fact that background features typically have
        lower spatial frequency than diffraction peaks. It iteratively smooths the image to obtain the background \cite{IvanovaMakowski1998}.
        Its performance depends on filter scale and the number of smoothing iterations. It may subtract weak, low-contrast signals.

        \textbf{Morphological Filter.}
        The white-top-hat morphological operation uses a structuring element
        to estimate and remove a slowly
        varying background, preserving smaller high-intensity features \cite{GonzalezWoodsDIP4e}.
        The size of the structuring element should be adjusted relative to the feature size.
        Smaller sizes may lead to the removal of larger low-contrast features.

        \textbf{Frequency-Domain Filtering.} 
        Fourier or wavelet transforms filter image components by spatial frequency. 
        Backgrounds are expected to have low frequency \cite{PolarBackground2024, WaveletBiomedOptExpress2021}.
        This is most effective for high-contrast defined features such as diffraction of crystalline samples.
        In fiber diffraction, features are more diffuse and may overlap with the background in their frequencies.

        \textbf{Kernel-Based Rolling Ball.} 
        Another method that performs well on crystalline patterns but less effective for fiber pattern is the \emph{Rolling Ball} technique, which 
        simulates a ball of specified radius rolling on the bottom of the intensity surface \cite{Sternberg1983}. 

    \subsubsection{Data-Driven Methods}
    These approaches learn the background subtraction mapping directly from the data.

        \textbf{Supervised Deep Learning.} Given significant amounts of labelled data, a neural network may be trained to perform background subtraction \cite{ArangurenCarmona2020}.
        However, in diffraction imaging, the lack of ground truth makes this approach challenging.
        
Choosing the appropriate method and parameters requires domain-expert qualitative assessment, 
which introduces subjectivity and risks the loss of subtle diffraction features.
A set of quantitative measures would assist in 
the comparison of the methods and their potential improvement. 


\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Background Subtraction Evaluation Principles}
\subsubsection{Qualitative Objectives} 

An ideal background removed image exhibits the following properties:
\begin{enumerate}
        \item Maximized Signal Contrast. True signal features are preserved with their original intensity,
        clearly distinguishable from the minimal near-zero background.
        \item No Artifact Introduction. The subtraction does not create artifacts, such as negative regions,
        or distort diffraction features in the data. The background should not contain any pattern that resembles
        diffraction features, such as layer lines or reflection peaks, indicating over-subtraction.
        Negative values should be minimized since they have no physical meaning in intensity data.
        \item Physically Plausible Residual Baseline. The remaining background in
        non-signal regions should be minimal, near-zero and smooth.
        \item Dataset Consistency. For images taken within the same experiment, 
        the background removal parameters and results should be similar across frames.
\end{enumerate}


\subsubsection{Quantitative Metrics}
\textbf{Direct Image Metrics.} Metrics that quantify properties of the background-removed image.
\begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Share of Negative Connected Components} ($\%\mathrm{Neg}_{\mathrm{con}}$): is the  percentage of negative pixels in

        this metric should be zero.
        \item \textbf{Share of Baseline Background Level} ($\%\mathrm{B}$):
        measures the percentage of pixels within a threshold
        distance from zero, reflecting minimal residual background.
        Successful background removal removes all structure while preserving natural noise.
        The threshold is based on the average intensity at the edge of the pattern, 
        where the background is expected to be minimal.

        \item \textbf{Mean Background Y-axis Derivative (Smoothness)} ($\mathrm{S}_{\mathrm{BG}}$):
        represents the average vertical change between successive rows.
        Smooth backgrounds produce minimal derivative values.
        Computed as $\mathrm{S}_{\mathrm{BG}} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_i | I(y_i + 1) - I(y_i)|$,
        across masked background rows to avoid known  signal features (see Section 3.2.1).
        Higher values indicate rougher backgrounds.
\end{itemize}



\textbf{Validation Using Synthetic Structures.} As part of the evaluation framework, we manually place synthetic signals into diffraction patterns to evaluate
the persistence of faint synthetic data after background removal (see Section 3.2.2). 

\begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Synthetic Signal Mean Squared Error} ($\mathrm{MSE}_{\mathrm{syn}}$):
        measures the
        difference between the background subtracted image
        and a reference containing only synthetic signal.
        Ideally, the value should be zero, reflecting perfect background subtraction with the preservation of features.
        Even though a mask is applied to exclude regions where real data location is known, the synthetic structures might overlap real features, increasing the metric.

        \item \textbf{Share of Negative Values After Synthetic Signal Subtraction} ($\%\mathrm{Neg}_{\mathrm{syn}}$):
        reflects if there are negative excursions when synthetic data is removed from the background subtracted image. 
        Ideally, the value should be zero, indicating no oversubtraction. 
        This is an additional component to $\mathrm{MSE}_{\mathrm{syn}}$ that
        emphasizes oversubtraction. 
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Implementation Framework}
\subsubsection{Region-Specific Masking}
Accurate metric calculation requires isolating regions containing signal from those dominated by background or artifacts.\
We use multiple level of masking to define these regions.

\textbf{Main Signal Mask} covers regions that a priori contain non-representative signal.
In fiber diffraction, this excludes the high-intensity center region from the beam and detector edges. It is an annular mask with
inner radius $r_{\mathrm{min}}$ and outer radius $r_{\mathrm{max}}$.

\textbf{Equator Background Mask} isolates equatorial region with strong horizontal equator background streak. 
In fiber diffraction patterns from muscle, the equatorial streak is more intense than
the rest of the general background and must be handled separately.
We use a rectangular mask aligned with the equator.

% On very low-intensity patterns or patterns from samples shot in air, where the 
% background is less intense and is concentrated around the center, this mask may be replaced with circular annular mask
% around the backstop center and a narrow equatorial band. 

\textbf{Synthetic Feature Mask} covers the areas where synthetic signal is added. Around each synthetic structure, we keep a rectangular area
of $2 \sigma$ in each direction, where $\sigma$ is the structure's standard deviation.

\textbf{Real Feature Mask} excludes regions where real data is expected. 
We mask out known layer line regions using horizontal bands centered around each layer line position; the width is determined
by assessing full width at half maximum (FWHM) of each layer line using perpendicular projections. 

The multi-layer masking helps focus background removal evaluation on background regions.


\subsubsection{Synthetic Signal Validation Design}

A fundamental challenge in evaluating background subtraction in fiber diffraction images is the lack of ground truth.
We aim to address this by injecting synthetic features that resemble real features into the diffraction images.


\textbf{Structural Shape Model.}
The feature shape is a 2D Gaussian function elongated along the horizontal axis:
$G(x, y) = A \exp(- \frac{(x-x_0)^2}{2\sigma_x^2} - \frac{(y-y_0)^2}{2\sigma_y^2} )$,
where 
$A$ is the amplitude, $(x_0, y_0)$ is the center, and $\sigma_x, \sigma_y$ control horizontal and vertical extent.
Typical shape of diffraction features is horizontally elongated, for example, layer lines, perpendicular to the meridian.

Shape parameters ($\sigma_x, \sigma_y$)
are chosen for each image automatically to adjust to the scale of each image and to
resemble real diffraction features.
Based on observed correlations in real diffraction features, we set $\sigma_x = \frac{I_{1,0}}{5}\times \frac{1}{2\sqrt{2 \ln 2}}$ and
$\sigma_y = \frac{\mathrm{M}1}{20}\times \frac{1}{2\sqrt{2 \ln 2}}$ based on
the average FWHM of layer line peaks and the positions of the most prominent diffraction features:
M1 and M3 meridional reflections and the $I_{1,0}$ equatorial peak.

% The shape values are user-chosen to resemble small diffraction features.


\textbf{Intensity Decay Model.}
The radial intensity decay model is $1/r$, where $r$ is the radial distance from the center.
The model reflects the scattering geometry of X-ray diffraction and comes from the Lorentz correction applied to 
intensity values to account for different amount of time that lattice points spend in diffracting positions.
For equatorial and meridional reflections with an incident beam normal to the sample the Lorentz factor can be reduced to $L \propto \frac{1}{\sin 2 \theta}$, 
where $\theta$ is the Bragg angle \cite{Buerger1940}.
Since at low angles $\theta$ is proportional to the distance of a diffraction feature from the center of the pattern ($r$),
a $1/r$ curve is a plausible approximation for the Lorentz correction \cite{Denny1995}.


\textbf{Amplitude Selection.}
We set the maximum amplitude in a way that at any specific radius it aligns with
the average intensity of real diffraction features at the same radius, using the $1/r$ decay model.
To select the maximum amplitude value for each image, we use the strongest diffraction feature value, which is the $I_{1,0}$ equatorial reflection.
Using  the $I_{1,0}$ reflection ensures that artificial data is applied to each image since even for faint images, the $I_{1,0}$ reflection should be present. 
Based on the evaluated data, setting the maximum artificial feature intensity as $A_{\mathrm{max}} = 1\%\ I_{1,0}$
aligns with the rest of the feature values and ensures faint features at high angles

\textbf{Sampling Frequency.}
Artificial structures are placed at regular intervals across the image to ensure full coverage of the image.
The sampling frequency $(f_x, f_y)$ determines placement density and potential overlap with layer lines.
The positions of the layer lines are taken 
into account as to avoid placing artificial structures on top of real data features and target background-dominated regions.

\subsection{Optimization Procedure}
\subsubsection{Aggregate Loss Function}
Loss is a weighted, multi-term function:
$$\begin{aligned}
L = w_1 \times S_{\mathrm{BG}}' + w_2 \times \%\mathrm{Neg}_{\mathrm{con}}' + w_3 \times   \%\mathrm{Neg}_{\mathrm{syn}}'\\
+ w_4 \times \mathrm{MSE}_{\mathrm{syn}}' + w_5 \times (1-\%\mathrm{B}')
\end{aligned}$$
% + w_5 \times \%\mathrm{Neg}'

Each metric is normalized by dividing by the average across the entire dataset.
The weights represent the priorities for optimization and could be adjusted to reflect user wishes. 
We compare the following strategies for weight selection: (1) equal weights with the ratio: $w_1 : w_2 : w_3 : w_4 : w_5 = 1 : 1 : 1 : 1 : 1 $,
(2) domain-informed weights with the ratio $w_1 : w_2 : w_3 : w_4 : w_5 = 3 : 3 : 2 : 2 : 1 $.
For the latter, higher ratios are given to metrics that enforce some physically meaningful constraints: (1)
smoothness and the share of connected negative values
are assigned higher weight due to direct physics violation, (2)
synthetic data metrics
should have moderate weight as they reflect data preservation, (3) the share of baseline values
should have low-to-moderate weight as it reflects the level of cleanup.

Visual assessment of residual background and resulting patterns is needed to manually adjust weights to detect
presence of negative excursions and the introductions of artifacts that shouldn't be in the diffraction pattern.
Introduction of unwanted artifacts is an aspect that is challenging to quantify and requires human verification. 
Based on visual evaluation, domain guided weights performed slightly better than equal weights; however,
they still need to be further adjusted to emphasize
the penalty for oversubtraction of faint synthetic features and enforce smooth background without oversubtraction
of layer lines (see Appendix Figure \ref{fig:example_wth_weight}). We chose to set the resulting weights as 
$[w_1, w_2, w_3, w_4, w_5] = [0.4, 0.1, 0.3, 0.1, 0.1]$. 

The effect of using each metric separately is shown in Appendix Figure \ref{fig:example_1img_metrics}.

\subsubsection{Parameter Optimization}

Using the defined loss function, we apply an optimization procedure to search for the optimal
parameter values for each of the background subtraction methods and compare them to each other.
Background removal algorithms typically involve non-differentiable operations
and discrete choices (e.g. filter window size, number of iterations etc.).
These operations have discontinuous gradients, therefore
direct search methods that evaluate only function values are a simpler choice. 
The downside is that these methods are computationally more expensive
as they may require many function evaluations to converge.

% We use the \emph{differential evolution} algorithm \cite{StornPrice1997} as implemented in the SciPy library \cite{SciPy2020}.
% This algorithm is a population-based stochastic optimization method that
% iteratively improves a population of candidate solutions based on mutation, crossover, and selection operations.
% We set the population size to 3 and the maximum number of iterations to 10, making the total
% number of function evaluations $30 \times n_{\mathrm{param}}$ per optimization run.
% The mutation strategy is `best1bin' and recombination parameters are set to 0.2 and 0.7.

We use a coordinate descent search to optimize each parameter separately, while the rest are frozen to the current best values. 
All the input parameters are either integers or show no qualitative difference with fractional parameter changes. 
A set of predefined initial values and bounds is kept for each background removal algorithm.
Based on the processed datasets, the resulting parameters for all current methods fall within the range [0,300],
which may be changed for other methods, depending on how the algorithms are implemented.
For all parameters, we use a range of step sizes from 50 to 1, and the step size decreases as the loss value improves. 
Parameters are processed in the order of significance. After the main optimization loop,
a small grid search is performed around the best parameters to further tune the parameters altogether.

For processing efficiency and improved runtime, images are downsampled before background removal
and upsampled for evaluation. Only the background is downsampled which is subtracted from the original image
to preserve information. The effect of downsampling varies for background subtraction methods
(see Appendix Tables \ref{tab:downsample_cs}, \ref{tab:downsample_sb}, \ref{tab:downsample_sg}, \ref{tab:downsample_wth}, \ref{tab:downsample_rw}).
Downsampling is applied when it assists in reducing the loss: (1)
downsampling with
a factor of 2 is applied to G-ILPF, B-ILPF, WTH; (2)
factor of 4 to RW; (3) no downsampling is applied to CS, RCH, AVG (See Section 3.4.1).

Multiprocessing is used to parallelize the processing of different files.

\section{Additional approaches}

\paragraph{Parametric General Background Model.}

Apart from the previously used non-parametric background removal methods evaluated in this work,
we explored a range of decay functions for the almost circularly symmetric model for the general background.
The isotropic particles should produce circular patterns, which is best described in polar coordinates.
In diffraction data, we observe that the general background is almost circularly symmetrical,
slightly elongated along the equatorial direction.

The parametric model for the general background that produced
plausible results is a combination of power law decay and orthogonal polynomials:
$B(q) \;=\; \sum_{j=0}^{3} a_j\,T_j(\tilde q)\;+\;A_{\text{p}}\,(q+q_0)^{-p}.$
This model is fitted to radial projections of the image using least squares optimization.
At the meridian, 2 projections are taken at $\pm 5^{\circ}$ to account for slight elongation along the equatorial direction.
One more projection is taken for the rest of the almost circular background to capture the average behavior.
After fitting the model to projections, a 2D background image is reconstructed and evaluated using the same approach.

Currently, this approach underperforms compared to the non-parametric methods evaluated in this work
due to oversubtraction, even though in the least squares fitting higher weight is placed on negative residuals.
(see Table \ref{tab:param_performance}).
In general, the parametric approach is less flexible and might introduce artifacts
in images where the background deviates from the assumed model. It however has the advantage of
avoiding overfitting to noise and preserving features. An example of a parametric background fit is shown in Figure \ref{fig:example_parametric}.

\paragraph{Equatorial Background.} \\

Muscle fiber diffraction patterns exhibit a strong equatorial streak which complicates background removal
due to its high intensity and non-uniform non-symmetrical shape. The only non-parametric method that can
successfully remove the equatorial streak is the 2D Radial Convex Hull (RCH) method; however, this approach tends to
introduce artifacts at higher angles due to the increased projection cone size. Combining RCH with other
methods can produce plausible results; however, it may create artifacts when transitioning between the two methods.

One parametric model for the equatorial streak background in diffraction patterns of polymers  \cite{MurthyZeroGrubb1997}
utilizes elliptical
coordinates and Pearson VII functions to fit the equatorial streak 
and lamellar reflections.
The rod-like particles present in both biological and polymer samples diffract in the shape of a streak
in reciprocal space. The approach may be applied to the equatorial streak in biological
fiber diffraction patterns due to the presence of similar equatorial streak and lamellar reflections.

Combining this approach with the general background removal described above requires the initial removal of the general
background to isolate the equatorial streak. The equatorial background is then fitted
using the described model and least squares minimization. Since 2D fitting is more computationally intensive, 
we downsample the pattern by a factor of 2 for processing efficiency.
After the equatorial streak is subtracted from the image,
the general background removed in the first step may be added back to run the optimization described
in Section 3.3. to choose the best general background removal method.

This approach allows plausible results on the whole pattern, including the equatorial region.
Fitting of the equatorial streak is sensitive to proper masking of the center beam region and layer lines, and this requires
operator intervention.
Figure \ref{fig:eq_2d_subtracted} shows examples of the resulting pattens after the
subtraction of the equatorial background and general background.

\paragraph{Additional Observations}
Using a guided filter to smooth the image before background estimation improves results
for non-parametric methods, in particular the WTH method that may introduce faint artifacts
due to the presence of noise. All the data remains preserved as the smoothing
is only applied to estimate the background, which is subtracted from the original image.

\section{Conclusion}

Using the described framework for the evaluation of the quality of background subtraction,
faint diffraction features can be preserved while the background is removed
from fiber diffraction patterns.
The optimization procedure selects the most suitable
background removal method and parameters for each pattern.
By combining this result with
parametric equatorial background fitting, it is possible to get excellent results across the entire 2D
pattern. Precise equatorial streak fitting at original resolution
can be computationally expensive and requires accurate masking of the intense center beam.
For many purposes, such as analysis of layer lines, removing only the general background may be sufficient.
Overall, the framework enables reliable automated background subtraction for fiber diffraction
and provides a foundation for further algorithmic improvements.


\section{Limitations and Future Directions}

The optimization framework is time-consuming due to the need to process images multiple
times to reach the global optimum. Capping the number of function evaluations is practical and
helps reduce runtime; however, it may reach suboptimal results.

Future work may focus on studying the evaluation metrics and individual algorithm parameters 
to understand their relationship to experimental conditions and physical meaning, and learn sample-dependent
differences in background. This understanding may potentially suggest
improvements to background subtraction algorithms.
