
Scientific studies in materials science and structural biology that rely on the analysis of diffraction patterns face the challenge of extracting weak signals overlapping high-intensity, complex and non-uniform backgrounds. The background arises from amorphous material within the sample, lattice disorder, instrumental artifacts, and scattering from the experimental environment and may obscure subtle diffraction features that are critical for quantitative analysis. X-ray fiber diffraction studies have provided unique insight into the sarcomere-level molecular structural dynamics of muscle and can contribute to understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying diseases ranging from nemaline myopathy to heart failure. To accurate interpret such diffraction patterns, background has to be effectively removed for reliable intensity measurements of diffraction features.

Muscle diffraction patterns have, in addition to the equatorial and meridional peaks and the continuous layer lines arising from the quasi-helical actin and myosin filaments, amorphous scattering from non-coherently diffracting material. In small-angle X-ray fiber diffraction experiments on muscle fibers, therefore, the signal of interest consists of diffraction peaks of varying intensity superimposed on a strong, spatially non-uniform diffuse background that must be removed prior to analysis.

Analysis of two-dimensional fiber diffraction patterns from partially crystalline structures such as muscle fibers using standard crystallographic techniques is challenging. The patterns show continuous bands of intensity along layer lines and meridional reflections on top of diffuse background scattering. As a result, fiber diffraction data are usually analyzed using one-dimensional projections which are effective for sharp reflections along the meridional and equatorial axes, where a large fraction of the signal is concentrated. However, one-dimensional projections are less suitable for studying continuous features such as layer lines, where separating signal from background is difficult. A robust approach for two-dimensional background subtraction would allow to extract structural information for features that are not analyzed systematically.

In diffraction studies, background removal strategy depends on the dimensionality of the data. Usually, background removal is performed on one-dimensional intensity profiles obtained by projection along directions of interest, usually the equator or the meridian. Diffraction peaks are often analyzed individually, in which case, the background intensity is estimated by averaging values in regions adjacent to the peak, which is then subtracted from the integrated peak intensity. This approach is simple and effective for individual reflections, but does not provide information on spatial variation in the background. Another commonly used technique is applying the convex hull algorithm to one-dimensional profiles to separate diffraction peaks from a smoothly varying background. The convex hull method allows to subtract high-intensity background, such as the equatorial streak; however, it is affected by background noise, which may introduce errors in measurements. Some parametric background models, such as low-order polynomials or spline functions are commonly used to remove slowly varying backgrounds, whereas steep exponential components may be used to model high-intensity variations. One-dimensional approaches are effective for sharp reflections on the equator or the meridian; however, the two-dimensional spatial information is inherently discarded. As a result, such methods provide limited information on continuous diffraction features such as layer lines.

Two-dimensional background subtraction methods are applied directly to diffraction patterns and preserve the spatial information. Before processing the image, a sample-free pattern may be subtracted to remove air scattering and instrumental artifacts. Most of two-dimensional approaches assume that the image is centered. Some methods are based on specific assumptions about the geometry or smoothness of the background. The radially symmetric method assumes that the background is symmetric about the beam and estimate it by averaging pixel intensities in concentric rings. The method is computationally efficient; however, this assumption does not hold for anisotropic background features, such as the equatorial streak characteristic of muscle fiber diffraction. Partition-based methods divide the pattern into local regions, estimate the background for each region, and interpolate between regions. Examples include the roving window method and the radial convex hull approach, which applies one-dimensional convex hull algorithm to angular projections. Although these methods can adapt to varying backgrounds, their performance depends on partition size and may introduce artifacts in regions with steep background gradients. In particular, the radial convex hull method tends to perform poorly at high scattering angles, where angular sectors span large areas of the pattern and the interpolation is no longer smooth.

Other methods utilize image processing techniques, exploiting differences in scale or frequency between background and features. Iterative low-pass filtering assumes that background is at lower spatial frequencies than the signal and estimates the background through repeated smoothing. This methods requires correctly chosen filter scale and the number of iterations. If filter is too large, weak diffraction features may be partially removed. The white top-hat morphological filter estimates the background using a structuring element and subtracts fine details smaller than the element from the original image, producing the background image. Performance depends on the size and the shape of the structuring element; inappropriate choices may oversubtract smooth low-intensity diffraction signals. Frequency-based approaches, such as Fourier or wavelet transforms, isolate low-frequency components as background. Such techniques are effective for crystalline diffraction patterns with sharp peaks, but perform poorly on fiber diffraction patterns, where signal and background overlap. Similarly, kernel-based rolling-ball method performs well on patterns from crystalline samples, but are less suitable for fiber diffraction data.

More recently, data-driven approaches have been explored. A supervised deep learning model has been used to learn muscle fiber diffraction backgrounds based on non-parametric method estimates. However, with fiber diffraction patterns, the lack of ground-truth background and the difficulty of generating representative training datasets limit the practical applicability of such approaches.

In current approaches to two-dimensional background removal, operator is required to visually assess subtraction quality and to adjust algorithm parameters. Manual processing introduces several challenges. Firstly, weak signals may be lost due to the large intensity contrast between background and features. Secondly, noise may obscure low-intensity features. Then, reliance on visual assessment introduces subjectivity, leading to suboptimal solutions. Finally, reproducibility depends on thorough documentation and reuse of chosen parameters. This leads to the question: can we find an optimal background-subtraction solution without ground truth? We argue that the absence of a standardized evaluation framework limits objective comparison of background subtraction methods and parameters.

This work presents a unified framework for improving the analysis of diffraction patterns that contain complex, non-uniform backgrounds. The framework replaces subjective visual assessment with physics-informed evaluation metrics, validation using synthetic signal, and automated parameter optimization. The main contributions are as follows:

Quantitative assessment of background subtraction quality given lack of ground truth. A set of quantitative metrics is derived from the properties of the signal generation process. These measures give anb objective reproducible way to assess background subtraction and allow for unsupervised comparison of algorithms.
Sampling framework for measuring fine detail preservation. Injection of faint synthetic diffraction features to prevent and penalize loss of weak signal.
Optimization procedure for background subtraction on muscle fiber diffraction patterns. A unified loss and optimization pipeline for automated method-specific parameter selection and automated method selection. The procedure has been incorporated in MuscleX, a software package for muscle fiber diffraction analysis, and is available for use.

One of the challenges in background subtraction for muscle fiber diffraction is the presence of a pronounced equatorial streak, arising from aligned cylindrical structures within muscle fibers, namely the actin-containing thin filaments and myosin-containing thick filaments. The equatorial streak is characterized by high intensity and anisotropic shape. The radial convex hull (RCH) approach has been most effective at subtracting the equatorial streak and produces visually plausible results at low angles. If the goal is the analysis of low angle data, RCH is often a good choice. However, RCH introduces artifacts at higher angles, where angular projection cones span larger areas of the pattern. The results demonstrate that the proposed optimization framework establishes a consistent and a more objective foundation for comparing background subtraction methods for different datasets. Since we evaluate the quality of background subtraction using quantitative metrics, different methods can be placed on a common scale, and the aggregate loss correlates with the expert visual assessment. This addresses the central limitation of current approach, where operator intervention and subjective assessment make results difficult to reproduce and compare.

No single background subtraction method consistently outperforms across metrics or datasets. The white top-hat filter has the lowest average loss; however, the iterative low-pass filters and the roving window method are competitive. No individual metric is sufficient to rank methods, which is the justification for the use of a composite loss: the metrics represent competing aspects that must be balanced.

The parametric and non-parametric approaches work in combination. The iterative two-stage fit removes the anisotropic equatorial streak and most of the general background, but by design leaves a residual baseline. Then, a non-parametric method cleans the remaining slowly varying component, resulting in the lowest loss, in both the general-background region and the equatorial region. This combination allows for background subtraction on the whole pattern, which is an improvement over existing approaches that usually work well only on either high or low angle regions of the pattern. A more accurate separation of background will allow for more accurate analysis of two-dimensional diffraction features and could contribute to future studies of how background structure changes with experimental conditions.

The appropriate choice of background subtraction strategy still depends on the data analysis goal: for low angles only, the RCH method is often efficient, sufficient and robust; for quantification of layer lines and higher angle meridionals, the equator may be masked and the optimization framework applied to the rest of the pattern; for background subtraction across the whole pattern, the iterative approach for the equatorial and general background fitting combined with the framework may be used.

Two limitations constrain these results. Firstly, optimized parameters vary substantially across datasets, and to a lesser extent within them. A single globally tuned parameter set is unlikely to be optimal; dataset or image subset specific tuning should be applied.Within-dataset spread also likely reflects parameter degeneracy, since different coefficient combinations can produce comparable loss values and background intensity. Secondly, the loss weights require operator judgment: the framework automates the parameter search but requires user input in how musch to penalize different aspects. Additionally, some artifacts, such as subtle layer line distortion, remain difficult to quantify. We recommend a hybrid workflow: run automated search, verify results on a small image subset, adjust weights, then process full dataset.


This section describes the methodological framework used to evaluate and optimize background subtraction methods for muscle fiber diffraction patterns. We define qualitative objectives and quantitative metrics that reflect meaningful properties of successful background removal. We then combine these metrics into a single loss to optimize each method's parameters. The study design and dataset selection is presented.

The evaluation framework is designed to provide objective, physically meaningful criteria for assessing the quality of background subtraction. This allows for reproducible comparison across methods and parameters.

A background subtracted diffraction pattern should fulfill several qualitative criteria. True diffraction features should be preserved witout distortion. Artifacts should not be introduced and negative excursions should be minimized. In regions with no diffraction signal, the residual should be a smooth, near-zero background. Finally, for patterns taken under similar conditions, background subtraction results and optimal parameters should be consistent across frames. These qualitative objectives motivate the following metrics in order to capture deviations from the described objectives in a reproducible way.

Several metrics operate directly on the background-subtracted image to evaluate the residual and whether artifacts were introduced. \textit{Oversubtraction} is assessed by the fraction of negative pixels in connected clusters larger than a specified minimum size (e.g.\ nine pixels). Random noise produces isolated negative pixels, whereas systematic oversubtraction results in connected regions. This metric, denoted $f_{\mathrm{neg,con}}$, is ideally zero. \textit{Residual background level} is calculated using the fraction of pixels with intensity within a specified threshold away from zero, denoted $f_{B}$. This metric reflects low residual while allowing for noise. The threshold is estimated using the average intensity near the edge of the pattern, where background is expected to be minimal. \textit{Background smoothness}, denoted $\mathrm{S}_{\mathrm{BG}}$, is calculated using the subtracted background pattern. Because ground-truth background is unknown for diffraction patterns, synthetic diffraction features are introduced to evaluate preservation of faint signal loss and oversubtraction. Preservation of synthetic signal, denoted $\mathrm{NMSE}_{\mathrm{syn}}$, is calculated using the normalized offset-invariant mean squared error. It is computed between the background-subtracted image and a reference image of only the injected synthetic features. Its value should be zero, indicating perfect preservation of synthetic signal. Although synthetic features are placed in regions known not to contain real diffraction features, if they overlap with real structure, $\mathrm{NMSE}_{\mathrm{syn}}$ increases. Oversubtraction of synthetic features, denoted $f_{\mathrm{neg,syn}}$, is measured using the fraction of negative pixels after synthetic signal subtraction. All metrics are summarized in Table.

We apply the $1/r$ model for the radial decay of the intensity of synthetic feature, where $r$ is the distance from the center. This choice is aligned with the scattering geometry of X-ray diffraction and approximates the Lorentz correction, which for equatorial and meridional reflections can be expressed as $L \propto 1/\sin 2\theta$. At small scattering angles, $\theta$ is approximately proportional to $r$, which makes $1/r$ a reasonable approximation. We set the amplitude of synthetic features to align with the intensity of real features. For each image, the amplitude is scaled based on the intensity of the $I_{1,0}$ and $I_{0,0}$ equatorial peaks, which are the strongest reflections. We set the maximum synthetic feature amplitude to $A_{\mathrm{max}} = 2\%\, I_{1,0} + 5\%\, I_{0,0}$ which makes the synthetic features faint yet still detectable. Synthetic features are placed on a regular grid to ensure broad coverage. The sampling frequency $(f_x, f_y)$ controls placement density and is chosen to avoid overlap with known diffraction features and layer lines.

Since the default settings resulted in low loss values, they were used for the subsequent analyses.


Accurate calculation of evaluation metrics requires to differentiate regions containing diffraction signal from background or artifact regions. To achieve this, several complementary masks are applied.  Main signal mask is an annulus that excludes non-representative signal: the beam center and detector edges. Equator mask is a horizontal band along the equatorial streak, which has a much higher intensity than the rest of the background. Synthetic mask consists of rectangles that extent $2\sigma$ around each injected synthetic feature.  Real-feature mask consists of horizontal bands at layer line positions. This mask is used to avoid contamination from real diffraction signal. The masks restrict metric evaluation to background regions and avoid other signal.

where the metrics are normalized by their mean values computed on a calibration subset. The weights $\lambda_i$ encode the relative importance and may be adjusted to reflect user priorities. We started off with equal weights and adjusted them based on visual inspection of the results. Although the optimization is driven by quantitative metrics, visual inspection remains necessary to adjust the weights and to identify artifacts that are difficult to capture numerically, such as faint negative excursions or distortion of layer lines. The final weights are nearly identical across datasets. The most common combination is $[\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3, \lambda_4, \lambda_5] = [0.1, 0.05, 0.05, 0.01, 0.8]$. Visualization of the results based on the optimization of individual metrics, shown in Appendix Figure, reflects that no single metric is sufficient to guide optimization.


The loss function is minimized to find optimal parameters for each subtraction method. Because these methods typically involve non-differentiable operations and discrete parameter choices (e.g.\ window size or number of smoothing cycles), gradient-based optimization is not well suited. Instead we may use direct search strategies that rely on function evaluations, despite their higher computational cost. To minimize $L$ we use a coordinate-descent strategy in which parameters are optimized sequentially, in the order of their estimated significance, with all other parameters held fixed at their current best values. Optimization uses a declining multi-resolution step-size schedule, with step sizes from 150 to 1. As the loss value improves, the step size is restarted from fine to coarse to avoid getting stuck in local minima. Images and methods are processed in parallel to lower runtime. Additionally, the background is estimated on a downsampled pattern; it is then subtracted from the full resolution image to preserve diffraction signal fidelity. Downsampling is applied only when it leads to reduced loss values: a factor of 2 is used for the background subtraction methods: G-ILPF, B-ILPF, RW, and WTH; and no downsampling for CS, RCH, or AVG.

This subsection describes an approach for jointly modeling the anisotropic equatorial streak and the surrounding isotropic general background. Fitting each component once is not sufficient as the components overlap and bias the other. For this reason, we iteratively refine the equatorial and general background fits, feeding each estimate into the other until they converge.



Each iteration round requires an initial estimate of the general background to isolate the equatorial streak for fitting. Scattering from isotropic components in diffraction experiments is expected to produce circular pattern, naturally described in polar coordinates. In muscle fiber diffraction data, the general background is observed to be nearly circularly symmetric, slightly elongated along the equatorial direction. Two-dimensional background is then interpolated and subtracted from the original image. This step ensures that the subsequent modeling of the equatorial streak is not biased by the isotropic background. This projection-based estimate, $B_0$, is used only to seed the first round of the iterative refinement described next; after this, it is replaced by the two-dimensional fit.

Parametric modeling of equatorial streaks has been developed for polymer diffraction data using elliptical coordinates and Pearson VII functions. Both polymer and biological samples with rod-like particles produce streak-like diffraction features in diffraction patterns, so the same model may be applied to muscle fiber diffraction. We model the equatorial streak with the elliptical-coordinate model and fit it by least squares. To speed up computation, we downsample the patterns twofold, then refine the equatorial steak and the general background jointly.

The general background is parameterized as an exponential decay plus a constant baseline plus one additional radially symmetric term, selected per image from a set of candidates: Lorentzian, power-law, and stretched-exponential. The form that reduces oversubtraction is selected for each image, since no single form was found to be best across all datasets. Such adaptive approach is useful when the goal is an accurate background fit. We presented a quantitative, physics-informed framework for the evaluation and optimization of background subtraction methods in muscle fiber diffraction images, replacing subjective visual assessment with reproducible metrics, synthetic-signal validation, and an automated parameter search. Out of the tested non-parametric background subtraction methods, no single method dominated universally, which is the central argument for a framework that selects and tunes methods per dataset rather than prescribing fixed algorithm and parameters.

Combining the iterative two-stage equatorial-general fit with a non-parametric framework method extends background subtraction across the whole pattern, including the equatorial streak, and improved on the RCH baseline in both the general-background region and the equatorial region itself, since the parametric and non-parametric approaches address different failure modes and are most effective combined rather than used in isolation.

Overall, the proposed framework establishes an objective and reproducible foundation for background subtraction in muscle fiber diffraction. Although the background removal is not fully automated, it is supported by a quantitative metric for evaluating subtraction quality. The new approach makes any required manual adjustment more intuitive, since the parameters have clear physical meaning. In addition, the system automatically searches over candidate algorithms and parameter values, reducing the extent of manual intervention needed.

The framework has been integrated into the MuscleX version 2.0.0, where it runs background subtraction with quantitative evaluation and parameter optimization as part of routine analysis while leaving users free to review results and make adjustments. While  this implementation is specific to muscle diffraction, the underlying metrics and optimization procedure are based on general signal and background properties, and the same approach could be extended to other fiber diffraction and imaging contexts where a structured, non-uniform background must be separated from weak signal without access to ground truth.
