\abstract{Accurate background subtraction is critical in the analysis of X-ray fiber diffraction patterns from striated muscle. Diffuse scattering and low signal-to-noise ratio complicate the separation of signal from background. Existing methods are applied heuristically, without guidance on parameter selection, leading to variability in following analyses. We propose a framework for background removal in muscle fiber diffraction images that evaluates methods and selects optimal parameters. It combines quantitative metrics and validation using synthetic features to guide the optimization. The aggregate loss and its components match expert judgment and allow to compare currently used approaches consistently. Individual metrics show trade-offs among approaches, such as preservation of detail or oversubtraction. Additionally, we evaluate parametric fitting of the equatorial streak and general isotropic background, which may be combined with the optimization framework. The framework improves the analysis of fiber diffraction patterns by providing an objective foundation for the choice of background subtraction method.}

\section{Introduction} 
\label{sec:introduction}

Scientific studies in materials science and structural biology that rely on the analysis of diffraction patterns face the challenge of extracting weak signals that overlap complex and non-uniform backgrounds. The background comes from the amorphous material within the sample, lattice disorder, and scattering from the experimental environment. It may obscure subtle diffraction features that are critical for quantitative analysis. X-ray fiber diffraction studies have provided unique insight into the sarcomere-level molecular structural dynamics of muscle and can contribute to understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying diseases ranging from nemaline myopathy to heart failure~\citep{MaIrving2022}. Interpretation of such diffraction patterns requitres accurate measurement of feature intensity.

Muscle diffraction patterns have, in addition to equatorial peaks, meridional peaks, and continuous layer lines arising from the quasi-helical actin and myosin filaments, amorphous scattering from non-coherently diffracting material. In small-angle X-ray fiber diffraction experiments on muscle fibers, the signal of interest consists of diffraction peaks of varying intensity superimposed on a strong, spatially non-uniform diffuse background that must be removed prior to analysis~\citep{squire2003ccp13_software}.

Analysis of two-dimensional fiber diffraction patterns from partially crystalline structures such as muscle fibers using standard crystallographic techniques is challenging. The patterns show continuous bands of intensity along layer lines and meridional reflections on top of diffuse background scattering. As a result, fiber diffraction data are usually analyzed using one-dimensional projections which are effective for sharp reflections along the meridional and equatorial axes, where a large fraction of the signal is concentrated. However, one-dimensional projections are less suitable for studying continuous features such as layer lines, where separating signal from background is difficult. A robust approach for two-dimensional background subtraction would allow us to extract structural information for features that are not analyzed systematically.

Background removal strategy in diffraction studies depends on the dimensionality of the data. Usually, when working with one-dimensional intensity profiles, diffraction peaks are analyzed individually. In this case, the background intensity is estimated by averaging values in regions adjacent to the peak, which is then subtracted from the integrated peak intensity~\citep{He2018}. This approach is simple and effective for individual reflections, but does not provide information on spatial variation in the background. Another commonly used technique is the convex hull algorithm~\citep{jarvis1973convex_hull} applied to one-dimensional profiles to separate diffraction peaks from a smoothly varying background~\citep{lee2019musclex}. The convex hull method allows us to subtract high-intensity background, such as the equatorial streak; however, it is affected by background noise, which may introduce errors in measurements. Some parametric background models, such as low-order polynomials or spline functions, may be used to remove slowly varying backgrounds, whereas steep exponential components may be used to model high-intensity variations~\citep{rajkumar2007fibrefix}. One-dimensional approaches are effective for sharp reflections on the equator or the meridian; however, the two-dimensional spatial information is inherently discarded. As a result, such methods provide limited information on continuous diffraction features such as layer lines.

Two-dimensional background subtraction methods are applied directly to diffraction patterns and preserve the spatial information. Before processing the image, a sample-free pattern may be subtracted to remove air scattering and instrumental artifacts. Most two-dimensional approaches assume that the image is centered. Some methods are based on specific assumptions about the geometry or smoothness of the background. The radially symmetric method assumes that the background is symmetric about the beam and estimates it by averaging intensities in concentric rings~\citep{rajkumar2007fibrefix}. The method is computationally efficient; however, this assumption does not hold for anisotropic background features, such as the equatorial streak characteristic of muscle fiber diffraction. Partition-based methods, such as the roving window method~\citep{rajkumar2007fibrefix}, divide the pattern into regions, estimate the background locally, and interpolate between regions. Another example is the radial convex hull approach, which applies the convex hull algorithm to angular projections~\citep{lee2019musclex} and then interpolates the two-dimensional background. The performance of these methods depends on partition size; they may create artifacts in areas with rapidly changing backgrounds. The performance of radial convex hull decreases at high angles because angular sectors cover larger portions of the pattern and the interpolation is no longer smooth.

Some methods utilize image processing techniques, for example, the iterative low-pass filtering method estimates the background through repeated smoothing~\citep{IvanovaMakowski1998}. The white top-hat morphological filter~\citep{GonzalezWoodsDIP4e} removes small details, using the result as background. For both methods, the performance depends on the size and the shape of the filter; inappropriate choices may oversubtract smooth low-intensity diffraction signals. Frequency-based approaches, such as Fourier or wavelet transforms, isolate low-frequency components as background. Such techniques are effective for crystalline diffraction patterns with sharp peaks~\citep{PolarBackground2024, WaveletBiomedOptExpress2021, renedecotret2017baseline}, but perform poorly on fiber diffraction patterns, where signal and background overlap. Similarly, the rolling-ball method~\citep{Sternberg1983} performs well on patterns from crystalline samples, but is less suitable for fiber diffraction data.

More recently, data-driven approaches have been explored. A supervised deep learning model has been used to learn muscle fiber diffraction backgrounds based on non-parametric method estimates~\citep{ArangurenCarmona2020}. However, with fiber diffraction patterns, the lack of ground-truth background and the difficulty of generating representative training datasets limit the practical applicability of such approaches.

In current approaches to two-dimensional background removal, the operator visually assesses the result and adjusts algorithm parameters. Manual processing introduces several challenges. Firstly, weak signals may be lost due to the large intensity contrast between background and features. Secondly, noise may obscure low-intensity features. Next, visual assessment introduces subjectivity and may result in suboptimal solutions. Finally, reproducibility depends on thorough documentation and reuse of parameters. We ask whether we can find an optimal solution to the background subtraction problem without ground truth. A lack of a standardized evaluation framework prevents objective comparison of background subtraction methods and parameters.

This work presents a framework for improving the analysis of diffraction patterns that contain complex, non-uniform backgrounds. The main contributions are as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Quantitative assessment of background subtraction quality given lack of ground truth}. A set of quantitative metrics is defined based on the properties of the expected background-subtracted patterns. These measures give a reproducible way to assess background subtraction and allow for unsupervised comparison of algorithms.
	\item \textbf{Sampling framework for measuring fine detail preservation}. Injection of faint synthetic features to penalize loss of signal.
	\item \textbf{Optimization procedure for background subtraction on muscle fiber diffraction patterns}. A unified loss and optimization pipeline for automated method-specific parameter selection and automated method selection. The procedure has been incorporated in MuscleX, a software package for muscle fiber diffraction analysis, and is available for use.
\end{enumerate}

The proposed framework provides solutions for better background subtraction and supports the development of future methodologies.

\subsection{Equatorial Streak}
\label{sec:equatorial_streak}

One of the challenges in background subtraction for muscle fiber diffraction is the presence of a pronounced equatorial streak, arising from aligned cylindrical structures within muscle fibers, namely the actin-containing thin filaments and myosin-containing thick filaments. The equatorial streak is characterized by high intensity and anisotropic shape. The radial convex hull (RCH) approach has been most effective at subtracting the equatorial streak and produces visually plausible results at low angles. If the goal is the analysis of low-angle data, RCH is often a good choice. However, RCH introduces artifacts at higher angles, where angular projection cones cover larger areas. Figure~\ref{fig:ch_example} shows an example of the equatorial streak background subtraction using the RCH method. Combining RCH with other methods can produce plausible patterns, but may add artifacts in the transition regions.

\section{Results}

This section summarizes the performance of the framework in finding an optimal solution to the problem of background subtraction given the lack of ground-truth background. The goal is to demonstrate how the framework provides a consistent way to compare and analyze methods using the aggregate loss function and the individual metrics, with lower values indicating better performance.

We first evaluate representative visual examples. Then, we demonstrate consistency of the outputs, followed by a comparison of the methods across datasets and analysis of optimized parameters. Finally, we evaluate the iterative fitting combined with the optimization framework, both visually and using the defined metrics.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The results demonstrate that the proposed optimization framework establishes a consistent and more objective foundation for comparing background subtraction methods for different datasets. Since we evaluate the quality of background subtraction using quantitative metrics, different methods can be placed on a common scale, and the aggregate loss correlates with the expert visual assessment. This addresses the limitation of current approaches, where the operator's subjective assessment makes results difficult to reproduce and compare.

No single background subtraction method outperforms others. The white top-hat method has the lowest average loss; however, the iterative low-pass methods and the roving window method are competitive. Individual metrics are not sufficient to guide optimization (Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:example_1img_metrics}), which emphasizes the need for a combined metric.

The parametric and non-parametric approaches work together. The iterative two-stage fit removes the anisotropic equatorial streak and most of the general background, but intentionally leaves a residual baseline. Then, a non-parametric method cleans the remaining slowly varying component, resulting in the lowest loss, in both the general-background region and the equatorial region. This combination allows for background subtraction on the whole pattern, which is an improvement over existing approaches that usually work well only on either high- or low-angle regions of the pattern. A more accurate separation of background will allow for more accurate analysis of two-dimensional diffraction features and could contribute to future studies of how background structure changes with experimental conditions.

The appropriate choice of background subtraction strategy still depends on the data analysis goal: for low angles only, the RCH method is often sufficient and robust; for quantification of layer lines and higher-angle meridionals, the equator may be masked and the optimization framework applied to the rest of the pattern; for background subtraction across the whole pattern, the iterative fitting approach combined with the framework may be used.

There are two main limitations. Firstly, optimized parameters vary significantly across datasets, and to some extent within datasets (Table~\ref{tab:summary_total_variability}). Dataset- or image-subset-specific tuning should be applied. This variability suggests multiple parameter combinations produce similar loss and integrated background intensity. Secondly, the loss weights require operator verification: the framework automates parameter search, but users must determine appropriate penalty levels for each loss component. Additionally, some artifacts, such as subtle layer-line distortion, remain difficult to quantify. We recommend a hybrid workflow: run an automated search, verify results on a small subset of images, tweak the settings, then process the full dataset.

\section{Materials and Methods}
\label{sec:approach}

This part explains the approach to assess background subtraction for muscle fiber diffraction patterns. We describe qualitative objectives and define quantitative metrics that reflect successful background removal. We then combine these metrics into a single loss that is used to optimize each method's parameters.


\subsection{Evaluation Framework}
\label{sec:evaluation_framework}

% The evaluation framework is designed to provide criteria for assessing the quality of background subtraction. 

\subsubsection{Qualitative Objectives}

A background-subtracted diffraction pattern should fulfill several qualitative criteria: preserve true diffraction features without distortion and avoid introduction of artifacts. In regions with no diffraction signal, the residual should be smooth and near-zero, and for patterns taken under similar conditions, background subtraction results and parameters should be consistent. These qualitative objectives motivate the following metrics in order to capture deviations from the described objectives in a reproducible way.

\subsubsection{Quantitative Metrics}

Some metrics use the background-subtracted image to evaluate residuals and detect artifacts. \textit{Oversubtraction}, denoted $f_{\mathrm{neg,con}}$, measures the fraction of negative pixels in connected clusters larger than a specified minimum (e.g.\ nine pixels). Its ideal value is zero. It excludes from the calculation isolated negative pixels caused by random noise. \textit{Residual background level} is calculated using the fraction of pixels with intensity within a small threshold around zero, denoted $f_{B}$. Low $f_{B}$ indicates minimal residual background despite noise. The threshold is estimated using the average intensity near the edge of the pattern, where background is expected to be minimal. \textit{Background smoothness}, denoted $\mathrm{S}_{\mathrm{BG}}$, is calculated using the background pattern. It is defined as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{S}_{\mathrm{BG}} =
\frac{1}{N} \sum_i \left| I(y_i + 1) - I(y_i) \right|,
\end{equation}
where summation is over rows in non-masked regions that exclude identified diffraction features. A smooth background should give a low value for this metric.

\subsubsection{Validation Using Synthetic Structures}

Because ground-truth background is unknown for diffraction patterns, synthetic diffraction features are added to quantify \textit{signal preservation}\ by computing $\mathrm{NMSE}_{\mathrm{syn}}$ (normalized, offset-invariant MSE). It is computed between the background-subtracted image and a reference image of only the injected synthetic features. Its value is expected to be zero, indicating perfect preservation of synthetic signal. Although synthetic features are placed in regions without real features, if they happen to overlap real structure, $\mathrm{NMSE}_{\mathrm{syn}}$ would increase. We measure \textit{feature oversubtraction}, $f_{\mathrm{neg,syn}}$ as the fraction of pixels that become negative after removing the added synthetic signal. All metrics are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:loss_terms}. 

\subsection{Sampling Framework for Assessing Detail Preservation}
\label{sec:sampling_framework}

This subsection describes the design of synthetic signal used for objective evaluation and region-specific masking used to isolate background regions for metric calculation.

\subsubsection{Synthetic Signal Validation Design}

To address the main challenge of missing ground-truth, we add synthetic diffraction features similar to real signal prior to background subtraction, as summarized in Algorithm~\ref{alg:synthetic_signal}. Synthetic features provide a controlled way to measure signal loss and oversubtraction. They are approximated using two-dimensional Gaussian functions elongated along the horizontal axis, given by
\begin{equation}
G(x, y) = A \exp\!\left(
-\frac{(x-x_0)^2}{2\sigma_x^2}
-\frac{(y-y_0)^2}{2\sigma_y^2}
\right),
\end{equation}
where $A$ is the amplitude, $(x_0, y_0)$ is the feature center, and $\sigma_x$ and $\sigma_y$ are the standard deviations. The shape parameters are selected automatically for each image to match its scale. The parameters $\sigma_x$ and $\sigma_y$ are approximated using the positions of the $I_{1,0}$ equatorial peak and the M1 meridional reflection:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_x = \frac{I_{1,0}}{5} \times \frac{1}{2\sqrt{2\ln2}}, 
\qquad
\sigma_y = \frac{\mathrm{M1}}{20} \times \frac{1}{2\sqrt{2\ln2}}.
\end{equation}
We apply the $1/r$ model for the radial decay of the intensity of the synthetic feature, where $r$ is the distance from the center. This choice approximates the Lorentz correction, which for equatorial and meridional reflections can be expressed as $L \propto 1/\sin 2\theta$~\citep{Buerger1940}, because at small scattering angles, $\theta$ is approximately proportional to $r$~\citep{Denny1995}. The amplitude of synthetic features is based on the intensity of the $I_{1,0}$ and $I_{0,0}$ equatorial peaks, which are the strongest reflections. We set the maximum synthetic feature amplitude to $A_{\mathrm{max}} = 2\%\, I_{1,0} + 5\%\, I_{0,0}$, which makes the synthetic features faint yet still detectable. Synthetic features are placed on a regular grid; the sampling frequency $(f_x, f_y)$ controls density and is chosen to avoid known diffraction features and layer lines.

\subsubsection{Region-Specific Masking}
\label{sec:masking}
Accurate calculation of evaluation metrics requires distinguishing regions with signal from background or artifacts. To achieve this, several complementary masks are applied. \textit{Main signal mask} is an annulus that excludes non-representative signal: the beam center and detector edges. \textit{Equator mask} is a horizontal band along the equatorial streak, which has a much higher intensity than the rest of the background. \textit{Synthetic mask} consists of rectangles that extend $2\sigma$ around each injected synthetic feature. \textit{Real-feature mask} consists of horizontal bands at layer-line positions; it is used to avoid contamination from real diffraction signal. The masks restrict metric evaluation specifically to background regions.

\subsection{Optimization Procedure}
\label{sec:optimization_procedure}

This subsection describes how the metrics are combined into an objective function, which is minimized to tune each method's parameters.

\subsubsection{Aggregate Loss Function}

The metrics are combined into a unified loss function, defined as
\begin{equation}
%\begin{aligned}
L =\;
\lambda_1\, S_{\mathrm{BG}}
+ \lambda_2\, f_{\mathrm{neg,con}}
+ \lambda_3\, f_{\mathrm{neg,syn}}
+ \lambda_4\, \mathrm{NMSE}_{\mathrm{syn}}
+ \lambda_5\, \bigl(1 - f_{B}\bigr),
%\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where the metrics are normalized by their mean values computed on a calibration subset. The weights $\lambda_i$ represent the importance of different aspects and may be adjusted. Visual inspection is needed to adjust weights and detect hard-to-quantify artifacts (e.g., layer-line distortion). In this study, the weights are similar for different datasets, with the most common combination being $[\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3, \lambda_4, \lambda_5] = [0.1, 0.05, 0.05, 0.01, 0.8]$. Visualization of the results based on the optimization of individual metrics, shown in Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:example_1img_metrics}, reflects that no single metric is sufficient to guide optimization.

\subsubsection{Parameter Optimization}

The loss function is minimized to find optimal parameters for each subtraction method using a coordinate-descent strategy in which parameters are optimized sequentially, in the order of their estimated significance, with all other parameters held fixed at their current best values. Since background subtraction methods typically involve non-differentiable operations and discrete parameter choices (e.g.\ window size or number of smoothing cycles), gradient-based optimization is not well suited. For this reason, we use direct search strategies that rely on function evaluations, despite higher computational cost. Optimization uses a declining multi-resolution step-size schedule, with step sizes from 150 to 1. As the loss value improves, the step size is restarted from fine to coarse to avoid getting stuck in local minima. Images and methods are processed in parallel to lower runtime. Additionally, the background is estimated on a downsampled pattern; it is then subtracted from the full-resolution image to preserve diffraction signal fidelity. Downsampling is applied only when it leads to reduced loss values: a factor of 2 is used for the background subtraction methods: G-ILPF, B-ILPF, RW, and WTH; and no downsampling for CS, RCH, or AVG.

\subsection{Iterative Two-Stage Background Subtraction}
\label{sec:two_stage}

This subsection describes an approach for jointly modeling the anisotropic equatorial streak and the surrounding isotropic general background. Fitting each component once is not sufficient as the components overlap and bias one another. For this reason, we iteratively refine the equatorial and general background fits, feeding each estimate into the other until they converge.

\subsubsection{Parametric Background Modeling}

Each iteration round requires an initial estimate of the general background to isolate the equatorial streak for fitting. In diffraction, scattering from randomly oriented components produces a circular pattern, which can be described in polar coordinates. In muscle fiber diffraction, the general background is nearly circularly symmetric, usually slightly elongated along the equatorial direction. The model that is used as the very initial fit combines a power-law decay with a low-order orthogonal polynomial expansion,
\begin{equation}
B(q) = \sum_{j=0}^{3} a_j\, T_j(\tilde q) + A_{\mathrm{p}}\, (q + q_0)^{-p},
\end{equation}
where $T_j$ denotes Chebyshev polynomials and $q$ is the radial coordinate. Parameters are estimated by least-squares to radial intensity projections between angular ranges $0-5^{\circ}$, $5-10^{\circ}$, and $10-60^{\circ}$ from the meridian. The two-dimensional background is then interpolated and subtracted from the original image. This step ensures that the subsequent modeling of the equatorial streak is not biased by the isotropic background. This projection-based estimate, $B_0$, is used only in the first round of the iterative refinement described in the next section.

\subsubsection{Equatorial Streak Modeling}

Parametric modeling of equatorial streaks has been developed for polymer diffraction data using elliptical coordinates and Pearson VII functions~\citep{MurthyZeroGrubb1997}. Both polymer and biological samples with rod-like particles produce streak-like diffraction features in diffraction patterns, so the same model may be applied to muscle fiber diffraction. We model the equatorial streak with the elliptical-coordinate model~\citep{MurthyZeroGrubb1997} and fit it by least squares.

\subsubsection{Iterative Refinement}

Figure~\ref{fig:two_stage_pipeline} summarizes the fitting procedure. In each iteration, we fit the equatorial streak $E_k$ on $\mathrm{img} - G_{k-1}$ and the general background $G_k$ on $\mathrm{img} - E_k$, using the result of the previous step. The general background is parameterized with three components: exponential decay, constant baseline, and an additional term chosen per image from Lorentzian, power-law, or stretched-exponential. The form that reduces oversubtraction is selected for each image, since no single form was found to be best across all datasets. An adaptive approach works best for an accurate background fit. The functional form can instead be kept fixed to study how the background shape changes with experimental conditions. The iterative refinement is done on smaller, downsampled images to speed up calculations. An example of the fitted backgrounds is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:visual_eq_none}.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a quantitative framework to evaluate and optimize background subtraction methods in muscle fiber diffraction images. It replaces subjective visual assessment with reproducible metrics, validation using synthetic signal, and an automated parameter search. Out of the tested non-parametric background subtraction methods, no single method outperformed the others, which is the central argument for a framework that selects and tunes methods per dataset.

Combining the iterative fitting procedure with a non-parametric method extends background subtraction across the whole pattern, including the equatorial streak region, and improves on the RCH baseline. The parametric and non-parametric approaches address different failure modes and are effective in combination.

Overall, the proposed framework establishes an objective foundation for background subtraction in muscle fiber diffraction. Although the process is not fully automated, it is supported by a quantitative metric for evaluating subtraction quality. The new approach makes any required manual adjustment more intuitive, since the parameters have clear physical meaning.

The framework and the iterative fitting have been integrated into MuscleX version 2.1.0~\citep{lee2019musclex}, where background subtraction may be applied with parameter optimization and quantitative evaluation, allowing the users to review results and make adjustments. Although the implementation is specific to muscle diffraction, the metrics and optimization procedure are based on general signal and background properties. A similar approach could be applied to other diffraction contexts where a non-uniform background must be separated from weak signal.
