\section{Introduction}
\subsection{Motivation}

Scientific imaging techniques across fields such as structural biology and materials science often face a common challenge:
extracting weak, structured signals from images dominated by complex, non-uniform backgrounds.
The backgrounds may arise from multiple sources, including sample disorder, instrumental artifacts, and scattering from
the surroundings and can obscure the subtle features necessary for quantitative analysis.

In techniques like small-angle X-ray scattering (SAXS), the signal of interest may consist of faint diffraction peaks
superimposed on a strong, non-uniform, diffuse background that needs to be removed prior to analysis~\cite{ResearchPlanMusico}.
SAXS is a technique that allows to evaluate nanometer scale density differences within a sample. 
In biology, it is used to study the interaction between macromolecules through the way they scatter X-rays in solution. 
The experiment involves using an X-ray beamline instrument with a high frame rate detector to collect time-resolution diffraction patterns.
% Muscle fiber diffraction patterns have weak diffraction peaks superimposed on a strong, non-uniform, diffuse background that needs to be removed prior to analysis~\cite{ResearchPlan}.
% The background in such patterns is due to scattering of the X-rays by amorphous material in the specimen, film fog, air scattering,
% and other instrumental effects. [5]

Diffraction data analysis is normally performed by taking one dimensional projections, following background removal and parametric peak evaluation.
Analysis of a two dimensional pattern as a whole using standard crystallographic techniques is challenging due to low resolution, high diffuse backgrounds, and overlapping information from multiple co-existing structures within a muscle sample. 
However, having a robust way of removing background directly in 2D patterns allows to evaluate structures globally.
Background removal is unique to this domain due to its non-uniform shape, structure and intensity. 

Current approaches for 2D background subtraction use non-parametric techniques, such as morphological filters or iterative smoothing.
These methods require subjective parameter selection and risk removing low-intensity
signal components, leading to biased or unreproducible results.
The absence of objective, physics-informed evaluation criteria complicates the development and comparison of improved algorithms.
Incorrect background estimation significantly impacts measured quantities in the image due to the intensity difference between the background and the peaks.

%  Such methods do not perform uniformly within an image due to local variations in the background, and thus need to be merged together introducing artificial transitions. 
%  Furthermore, existing techniques require selecting parameters, often manually, and in this way, introduce bias into the background subtraction process. 
% An accurate background removal that does not remove faint details will allow to find new information in the diffraction patterns and study it. 

Addressing the general problem of reliable separation of weak structured signals
from complex backgrounds would enhance data fidelity
across multiple imaging domains by establishing a
foundation for systematic methodological progress.

\subsection{Problem Statement}
The separation of signal patterns from a dominant background is a fundamental problem in quantitative imaging.
In domains like material science or structural biology, diffraction analysis
requires the isolation of faint diffraction peaks from a non-uniform diffuse scattering background. 
Current approaches rely on operator intervention in the 1. visual assessment of subtraction quality
and the 2. manual adjustment of algorithm parameters.

Manual processing introduces several challenges: 
\begin{itemize}
        \item Subjectivity: qualitative assessment leads to a plausible, yet suboptimal solution.
        Without quantitative metrics, reproducing results requires saving of user-adjusted parameters, hindering scientific rigor;
        \item Loss of Weak Signal: the high intensity range between background and peaks as well as the interference of noise mask faint data, resulting in potentially removing low-intensity structural elements.  
\end{itemize}

% in the image as well as the interference of noise. Additionally, the preservation of small details in an image after background removal is challenging using a manually supervised 
% workflow due to the high intensity difference between the background and the peaks. 
% The lack of an evaluation criteria results in the operator having to manually decide on a plausible, 
% yet suboptimal background removal procedure, which is not reproducible unless the user-adjusted input parameters are saved. 

The field lacks a standardized foundation to evaluate and optimize background subtraction methods.
This gap impedes the development of better algorithms and limits the extraction of reliable data.

\section{Related Work}

% The accurate separation of signal from background in diffraction patterns is a foundational step
% for quantitative analysis.
Existing methods for background removal depend on the dimensionality of the data
and the nature of the sample. Below is an overview of 1D profile techniques and
2D background removal methods.

\subsection{1D Profile Peak Analysis}

Normally, when analyzing the integrated intensity of diffraction peaks, 
specific regions are analyzed separately and background is removed by taking the weighted average of the regions adjacent
(lower and higher) to the diffraction peak and subtracting from the main integrated intensity. \cite{NederKorsunskiy2018}
Another common approach is to apply the convex hull algorithm to the 1D intensity profile to model the overall envelope
of the intensity allowing to discard the shape underneath as background.
It is a robust approach, but sensitive to noise.
Alternatively, functional fitting may be used to fit the background in 1D projections.
Pearson VII is a common choice in diffraction data due to its flexibility \cite{NA}.,
however, it can produce multiple solutions to a similar fit. \cite{Zorn1988}

While effective for isolated peaks, the 1D techniques don't take into account the spatial context of the original 2D pattern,
motivating the use of 2D background removal methods.

\subsection{2D Pattern Background Removal Methods}

Direct 2D background removal techniques preserve global information and
allow for more sophisticated analysis.
These methods can be broadly categorized by the underlying algorithmic strategy.
In fiber diffraction images, prior to background removal, 
an experimental correction step should be done to subtract
a sample-free measurement from the pattern,
thus removing instrumental artifacts and air scattering.

    \subsubsection{Assumption-Based Methods}
        Such techniques are based an an assumption about the pattern's symmetry or background parametric model.

        \textbf{Radial Symmetry Model.} For fiber diffraction patterns, a common and fast approach assumes the background
        varies primarily with radial distance from the beam center.
        The \emph{Circularly-Symmetric} method averages pixel intensities in concentric rings to generate a smooth,
        radially dependent background model. Its main limitation is the inability to handle non-radially symmetric features,
        such as an intense equator streak. \cite{FibreFixDiamond}

        \textbf{Partitioned Interpolation.}
        Such methods divide the pattern into segments, estimates background locally,
        and interpolates to form a global surface.
        The \emph{Roving Window} method scans windows across the image,
        identifying and averaging background pixels,
        and then smooths the result into a continuous surface \cite{LanganRowingWindow}. 
        Similarly, the \emph{Radial Convex Hull}
        method projects the 2D pattern along fine angular intervals,
        applies a 1D convex hull subtraction to each projection,
        and interpolates the results back into 2D \cite{MuscleXQuadrantFolding}.
        These methods are adaptable but
        their performance depends on the choice of
        partition size,
        which can introduce artifacts in high-gradient regions.

        \textbf{Parametric Surface Fitting.} Background can be modeled as a smooth 2D function,
        such as an even-ordered polynomial or a finite double Fourier-Bessel series, fitted to the data 
        \cite{LorenzHolmes1993, MillaneArnott1985, Makowski1978}.
        While effective for slowly varying backgrounds, these models struggle with the complex,
        rapidly varying backgrounds typical of biological samples.

    \subsubsection{Filter and Transform-Based Methods}
        These approaches perform background removal as an image processing task,
        leveraging such properties as local contrast or spatial frequency.

        \textbf{Multi-Scale Filtering.}
        The iterative low-pass filtering technique uses the fact that background features typically have
        lower spatial frequency than diffraction peaks.
        Through repeated cycles of smoothing and subtraction, it separates fine signal features from the underlying background \cite{IvanovaMakowski1998}.
        Its performance is sensitive to the choice of filter scale and iteration count, risking over-subtraction of broad,
        low-contrast signals.

        \textbf{Morphological Filter.}
        The white-top-hat morphological operation uses a structuring element
        to estimate and remove a slowly
        varying background, preserving smaller, high-intensity features. \cite{GonzalezWoodsDIP4e}
        The method requires careful tuning of the structuring element
        relative to feature sizes and is sensitive to noise,
        which can lead to the erosion of large
        or low-contrast structures and the introduction of artifacts.

        \textbf{Frequency-Domain Filtering.}
        Applying a Fourier (or wavelet) transform separates image components by spatial frequency.
        Low-frequency components are associated with the background and can be attenuated or removed
        \cite{PolarBackground2024, WaveletBiomedOptExpress2021}.
        This method is most effective for patterns with sharp, high-contrast features (e.g., crystalline samples)
        but is less suited for amorphous or biological diffraction patterns where signal and background occupy
        overlapping frequency bands.

        \textbf{Kernel-Based Rolling Ball.} A well established techniques for images with sharp peaks is
        the Rolling Ball algorithm, which simulates a ball of a defined radius rolling beneath the intensity surface \cite{Sternberg1983}.
        The surface traced by the ball is subtracted as background.
        It is highly effective for distinct peaks on a smooth baseline but fails when background variations
        are on a scale similar to or larger than the chosen ball radius.

    \subsubsection{Data-Driven Methods}
    Emerging approaches leverage machine learning to learn the background subtraction mapping directly from data.

        \textbf{Supervised Deep Learning.} Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) can be trained on paired datasets
        of raw and background-subtracted images to perform the subtraction.
        The major limitation is the requirement for high-quality, representative training data. In scattering diffraction patterns,
        the lack of definitive ground truth makes generating training sets challenging.
        
The overarching limitation is the absense of an objective evaluation framework. Choosing between the methods 
or deciding on the parameter values, the domain expert operator must rely on quanlitative assessment and manual parameter adjustment. 
This introduces subjectivity and risks the loss of less noticiable data. The lack of quantitative benchmarks makes it difficult
to compare methods between one another or to improve upon them in a systematic way.


\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Principles for Assessing Background Subtraction}
\subsubsection{Qualitative Objectives} 
The main objective of background subtraction is to isolate the signal of interest by removing a spatially varying baseline.
An ideal result exhibits three key properties:
\begin{enumerate}
        \item Maximized Signal Contrast: True signal features are preserved at their original intensity and spatial profile,
        standing clearly against the corrected baseline.
        \item Minimized Artifact Introduction: The subtraction process does not create new structures (e.g., negative imprints)
        or distort existing ones. The background, once removed, should not contain patterns that resemble
        the main features (e.g. layer lines, reflection peaks). Negative intensity values in the residual image
        should be minimal in quantity
        and magnitude as they should only be attributed to random noise, since negative values have no physical meaning in intensity data.
        \item Physically Plausible Residual Baseline: The remaining background in non-signal regions should be minimal and
        smooth. %, and statistically consistent with noise.
        In scattering experiments like X-ray diffraction, this corresponds to discrete peaks and layer lines on a
        featureless background, with near-zero intensity values. 
        \item Dataset Consistency: For images taken under similar conditions, 
        the background removal parameters and results should
        produce visually and quantitatively similar outputs across frames.
\end{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Share of Baseline Background Level} ($\%\mathrm{B}$)
        measures the proportion of pixels within a threshold
        distance from zero,
        indicating minimal residual background.
        Successful background removal removes all structure while preserving natural noise.
        The threshold is based on the average intencity at the edge of the pattern, 
        where the background is expected to be minimal.
        It is set to $20\%$ of the average intensity at the highest radial distance
        or a minimum value of 1 to account for low-intensity images.

        \item \textbf{Mean Background Y-axis Derivative (Smoothness)} ($\mathrm{S}_{\mathrm{BG}}$)
        is the mean of the first-order vertical difference.
        Smooth backgrounds produce low derivative values,
        while patterns or residual features produce higher values.
        $\mathrm{S}_{\mathrm{BG}} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_i | I(y_i + 1) - I(y_i)|$,
        where the sum is over rows in masked background regions to exclude known signal features (see Section 3.2.1).
        Higher values indicate rougher (worse) backgrounds.

\begin{itemize}

        synthetic structures

        puts more emphasis on oversubtraction. This metric should be minimized aggressively since
        negative values have no physical meaning in intensity data.

\subsection{Implementation Framework}
\subsubsection{Region-Specific Analysis via Masking}
Accurate metric calculation requires isolating regions containing signal from those dominated by background or artifacts.
We employ a multi-stage masking strategy to define these regions programmatically.

\textbf{Primary Signal Mask} identifies regions known apriori to contain dominant, non-representative signal.
In X-ray diffraction, this excludes the intense central scatter and detector edges and is defined
as an annular mask with inner radius $r_{\mathrm{min}}$ and outer radius $r_{\mathrm{max}}$.

\textbf{Anisotropic Background Mask} isolates regions with strong directional background structures
for exclusion from global background analysis.
In fiber diffraction patterns, the equatorial streak is masked out as
it contains extremely intense scattering from the specimen's lattice and
can dominate
background intensity.
For this region,
we use a rectangular mask aligned with the equator,
where the mask height is determined by the equatorial peak width.

\textbf{Synthetic Signal Mask} is defined in regions where synthetic signal is inserted to localize measurement.
In practice, for fiber diffraction data, around each synthetic structure, we retain a rectangular region extending
$2 \sigma$ in each direction, where $\sigma$ is
the structure's standard deviation in a particular dimension.

\textbf{Feature Mask} is used in regions where the signal is known and expected. 
In diffraction patterns, we mask out layer line regions using horizontal band mask centered around known layer line positions.
The width of the band is determined by assessing full width at half maximum (FWHM) 
using perpendicular projections. 

This multi-layer approach ensures metrics reflect the algorithm's performance on the relevant image components.

\textbf{Rationale:}
A fundamental challenge in evaluating background subtraction is the lack of ground truth.
We address this by injecting synthetic signal features with controlled properties into real background-dominated images.
The design of such features is informed by the statistical characteristics of the true signal within the target domain.

\textbf{Structural Shape.}
We use a 2D Gaussian function elongated along the horizontal axis:\\
$ G(x, y) = A \exp(- \frac{(x-x_0)^2}{2\sigma_x^2} - \frac{(y-y_0)^2}{2\sigma_y^2} ))$,
where 
$A$ is amplitude, $(x_0, y_0)$ is the center, and $\sigma_x, \sigma_y$ control horizontal and vertical extent.
An elongated shape reflects the anisotropic nature of diffraction features,
where peaks often extend more in horizonal direction (e.g., layer lines) than vertical.

To select the shape parameters ($\sigma_x, \sigma_y$)
for each image automatically, real diffraction features were evaluated across multiple images.
We set $\sigma_x = \frac{I0}{5}\times \frac{1}{2\sqrt{2 \ln 2}}$ and
$\sigma_y = \frac{M1}{20}\times \frac{1}{2\sqrt{2 \ln 2}}$ based on
the average FWHM of layer line peaks to the location of the most prominent diffraction features:
M1 and M3 layer lines and the I0 equatorial peak. These values guarantee
that the artificial structures resemble real diffraction features in size and shape and adjust to the scale of each image. 

% $M1$ is the distance between the center and the M3 peak divided by 3 and $I0$ is the position of the I0 equatorial peak.



\textbf{Intensity Decay Model.}
The radial $1/r$ intensity decay reflects the scattering geometry of X-ray diffraction.\
This decay model approximates
the real experimental setup
where pattern intensity decreases with distance from the scattering center.
% \colorbox{blue!10}{Cite sources}

\textbf{Amplitude Selection.}
We set the amplitude such that at a specific radius, it aligns with
the average intensity of real diffraction peaks at the same radius.
Based on the observed data, setting the artificial feature intensity to around 15\% \
of the average image intensity closest to the edge is reasonable and aligns with the rest of the feature values. 
We use 30\% to ensure that the synthetic structures are faint yet
above the noise level. The minimum intensity value is set to 1 to account for faint images.
The amplitude at the center of the pattern is then calculated using the $1/r$ decay model.

\subsection{Optimization and Comparative Evaluation}
\subsubsection{Aggregate Loss Function and Weight Tuning}
Loss is a weighted, multi-term function:
$$\begin{aligned}
L = w_1 \times S_{\mathrm{BG}}' + w_2 \times \%\mathrm{Neg}_{\mathrm{con}}' + w_3 \times   \%\mathrm{Neg}_{\mathrm{syn}}'\\
+ w_4 \times \mathrm{MSE}_{\mathrm{syn}}' + w_5 \times (1-\%\mathrm{B}')
\end{aligned}$$
% + w_5 \times \%\mathrm{Neg}'

Each metric is normalized to a common scale by diving by the average across the dataset. 
The weights encode the priorities of the analysis, incorporating domain knowledge. 
We compare two strategies for weight selection: 1. Equal Weights, 2. Domain-Informed Weights.
Higher weight is assigned to metrics that directly enforce physically meaningful constraints.
Smoothness and negative values are assigned higher weight due to direct physics violation ($w_1, w_2$).
Synthetic data metrics should have moderate weight as they reflect data preservation ($w_4,  w_3$).
Baseline values should have low-to-moderate weight as it reflects cleanup level ($w_5$). 

The initial weight are set with the following ratio: $w_1 : w_2 : w_3 : w_4 : w_5 = 3 : 3 : 2 : 2 : 1 $.\\
Domain-guided weighs are set with the following ratio: $w_1 : w_2 : w_3 : w_4 : w_5 = 3 : 3 : 2 : 2 : 1 $.

Based on the visual evaluation of the resulting images (see Appendix),
the domain-informed weights produced better results and were selected for the final evaluation.
Further hyperparameter tuning should be performed (for example, using Pareto front analysis).

\subsubsection{Parameter Optimization}

Using the defined loss function, we apply an optimization procedure to search for the optimal
parameter values for each of the background subtraction methods and compare them to each other.
Background removal algorithms typically involve non-differentiable operations
and discrete choices (e.g. filter window size, number of iterations etc.).
These operations have discontinuous gradients, therefore
direct search methods that evaluate only function values are an easier choice. 
The downside is that these methods are computationally more expensive
as they require many function evaluations to converge.

We use the \emph{differential evolution} algorithm \cite{StornPrice1997} as implemented in the SciPy library \cite{SciPy2020}.
This algorithm is a population-based stochastic optimization method that
iteratively improves a population of candidate solutions based on mutation, crossover, and selection operations.
We set the population size to 3 and the maximum number of iterations to 10, making the total
number of function evaluations $30 \times n_{\mathrm{param}}$ per optimization run.
The mutation strategy is `best1bin' and recombination parameters are set to 0.2 and 0.7.


\subsubsection{Validation via Lower-Dimensional Projections}

After 2D optimization, we are able to collapse the 2D data along a key dimension to use established 1D analysis technique,
such as peak fitting, 
as an independent check. For fiber diffraction data, we are able to perform the following validations: 
\begin{itemize}
        \item Equatorial Projection: Extract the equatorial region (horizontal line through the scattering center)
        from the background-removed 2D image. Integrate intensities vertically to create a 1D profile.
        Fit this profile to a model representing expected equatorial reflections and measure goodness-of-fit.
        \item Meridional Projection: Extract the meridional region (vertical line) and integrate horizontally to create a 1D profile.
        Layer lines appear as regular peaks spaced the axial repeat period.
        Fit a mixture of Gaussians with fixed means (at known layer line positions) but free amplitudes and widths measure goodness-of-fit.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Parametric Background Models}


Apart from the widely used non-parametric background removal methods evaluated in this work,
we also explored parametric background modeling approaches.
We evaluated two main parametric models:
an almost circularly symmetric model for the general background with different decay functions,
and an elliptical model for the equatorial streak based on the model applied
in material science for the diffraction patterns of polymers \cite{MurthyZeroGrubb1997}.


The isotopic particles (general background) should produce circular patterns, 
which is best described in polar coordinates. In diffaction data,
we observe that the general background
is almost circularly symmetrical, slightly elongated along the equatorial direction.

The parametric model for the general background that produced
plausible results is a combination of power law decay and orthogonal polynomials:
$B(q) \;=\; \sum_{j=0}^{3} a_j\,T_j(\tilde q)\;+\;A_{\text{p}}\,(q+q_0)^{-p}.$

It underperforms compared to the non-parametric methods evaluated in this work (see Table \ref{tab:param_performance}).
The parametric approach is less flexible and might introduce artifacts
in images where the background deviates from the assumed model.

\subsection{Observations}
Using a guided filter to smooth the image before background estimation improves results for non-parametric methods.
All the data remains preserved as the the smoothing
is only applied to estimate the background, which is subtracted from original image.

\section{Limitations and Future Directions}

The optimization framework is time consuming due to the need to process images multiple
times to reach global optimum. Current background removal methods are computationally intensive.
Setting the threshold on the number of function evaluations helps reduce computation time;
however, it may result in suboptimal solutions. Work is needed to explore more efficient
optimization techniques
suitable for non-differentiable functions.

Algorithm parameters and metrics should be studied
to understand their physical meaning and relationship to experimental conditions.
This may provide insights into how experimental setups influence background characteristics
and guide the design of better background removal algorithms.

Given quantitative measures of background removal quality, future work may explore
developing new background removal algorithms that optimize these metrics.
Machine learning approaches, such as reinforcement learning or evolutionary algorithms,
could be employed to learn optimal background subtraction strategies from data.
