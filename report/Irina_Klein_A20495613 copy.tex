\begin{synopsis}
A quantitative framework is presented for evaluating and optimizing background subtraction methods for synchrotron X-ray fiber diffraction patterns from striated muscle.
\end{synopsis}


%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\begin{abstract}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Accurate background subtraction is a critical preprocessing step for quantitative analysis of X-ray fiber diffraction patterns from striated muscle but is often performed heuristically, with limited guidance for parameter selection, leading to variability in downstream quantitative analyses. We present a unified framework that systematically evaluates and optimizes background subtraction methods using physically motivated image-quality metrics and synthetic signal validation to perform automated parameter search. Applied to synchrotron X-ray muscle fiber diffraction datasets, the framework improves subtraction consistency relative to ad hoc settings while preserving key diffraction features such as equatorial reflections and layer lines, and the resulting aggregate loss correlates with expert visual assessment and highlights characteristic failure modes of different subtraction strategies. Although developed for striated muscle diffraction, the approach is based on general signal and background properties and can be extended to other classes of fiber diffraction data. By formalizing evaluation and optimization, it supports more reliable quantitative analysis of synchrotron diffraction experiments.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Reliable background subtraction is essential for quantitative analysis of muscle fiber diffraction patterns but remains challenging because strong, non-uniform scattering can obscure weak diffraction features.

\subsection{Motivation}

Scientific imaging techniques used in structural biology and materials science that rely on diffraction analysis often face a common challenge: extracting weak diffraction signals from images dominated by complex, non-uniform backgrounds, which arise from amorphous regions within the sample, lattice disorder, instrumental artifacts, and scattering from the experimental environment. Together, these contributions complicate quantitative analysis. One situation where these concerns are particularly relevant is the case of fiber diffraction from striated muscle.

X-ray fiber diffraction provides unique insight into the sarcomere-level molecular structure of muscle and has proven valuable for studying the structural consequences of cardiac and skeletal myopathies. Analysis of muscle diffraction patterns can therefore contribute to understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying diseases ranging from nemaline myopathy to heart failure~\cite{MaIrving2022}. Accurate interpretation of these diffraction patterns requires reliable intensity measurements and effective background subtraction. Muscle diffraction patterns have, in addition to the equatorial and meridional peaks and the continuous layer lines arising from the quasi-helical actin and myosin filaments, amorphous scattering from a large fraction of non-coherently diffracting material. In small-angle X-ray fiber diffraction experiments on muscle fibers, therefore, the signal of interest consists of diffraction peaks of varying intensity superimposed on a strong, spatially non-uniform diffuse background that must be removed prior to analysis~\cite{squire2003ccp13_software}.

Historically, fiber diffraction data have been analyzed mostly using one-dimensional projections followed by background removal and parametric peak evaluation. Such one-dimensional analyses are effective for sharp reflections along the meridional and equatorial axes, where a large fraction of the signal is concentrated. However, they are less suitable for studying continuous features such as layer lines, where distinguishing diffraction signal from background is inherently difficult. A robust approach to background removal applied directly to two-dimensional diffraction patterns would therefore enable more comprehensive extraction of structural information, including features that are not typically analyzed systematically.

Existing two-dimensional background subtraction methods commonly rely on non-parametric techniques, such as morphological filtering or iterative smoothing. These approaches typically require manual parameter selection and may inadvertently remove weak diffraction signals. Because the intensity of the background often exceeds that of the signal by orders of magnitude, even small errors in background subtraction can significantly affect downstream quantitative analysis. An objective, physics-informed framework for evaluating background subtraction performance would facilitate systematic comparison of existing methods and support the development of improved approaches. More broadly, such approaches towards reliable separation of weak signals from complex backgrounds have the potential to enhance data fidelity across a wide range of scientific imaging applications.


\subsection{Problem Statement}

The separation of signal from background is a fundamental problem in quantitative imaging. In current approaches to background removal for muscle fiber diffraction patterns, operator intervention is typically required to visually assess subtraction quality and to manually adjust algorithm parameters. Such manual processing introduces several challenges. First, weak diffraction signals may be lost due to the large intensity contrast between background and peaks, combined with the presence of noise that can mask low-intensity features. Second, reliance on visual assessment introduces subjectivity, leading to solutions that may be plausible but not optimal. Finally, reproducibility depends on careful documentation and reuse of user-selected parameters. The absence of a standardized, quantitative evaluation framework limits the ability to objectively compare background subtraction algorithms and impedes systematic parameter optimization. This lack of objective assessment hinders reproducibility and slows methodological progress.


\subsection{Scope}

This work presents a unified framework for improving the analysis of weak diffraction signals in the presence of complex, non-uniform backgrounds. The framework replaces subjective visual assessment with quantitative, physics-informed evaluation criteria and combines these metrics with synthetic-signal validation and automated parameter optimization. The main contributions are as follows:


\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{System of Objective Evaluation Measures.} A set of physics-informed metrics for reproducible, unsupervised comparison of background-subtraction results.
\item \textbf{Faint Detail Preservation.} Injection of faint synthetic features to penalize signal loss during subtraction.
\item \textbf{Benchmarking and Optimization Framework.} A unified loss and optimization pipeline for method-specific parameter selection.
\end{enumerate}

Although developed for muscle diffraction, the framework is applicable to other imaging problems with complex, non-uniform backgrounds.

The framework addresses the need for improved background subtraction using existing methods while also providing a tool for ongoing methodological development in quantitative image analysis. \section{Related Work}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Background removal strategies in diffraction imaging depend strongly on both the dimensionality of the data and the structural characteristics of the sample. At one extreme is macromolecular solution scattering (SAS) where the scattering signal is isotropic arising from typically only two components, the macromolecule and the solvent. The background subtracted scattering signal from the macromolecules is simply the scattering from solution minus the scattering of the solvent alone. Data reduction packages for macromolecular SAS data are well developed and include the ATSAS package~\cite{franke2025atsas4} and BioXTAS~Raw~\cite{hopkins2024bioxtas_raw2}. SASview provides similar functionality for materials science applications~\cite{krzywon2025sasview612}. At the other extreme from SAS is macromolecular crystallographic data, where the samples have three-dimensional crystallographic ordering and diffract to high resolution. Amorphous regions are minimal and the solvent yields relatively flat and featureless scattering that can be removed with simple local operations. In this domain, there have been many software packages developed, including the well-known community-supported CCP4 package~\cite{agirre2023ccp4_suite} as well as others.

Fibrous systems lie between the two extreme cases, having longitudinally aligned assemblies of rod shaped molecules but otherwise lack three-dimensional crystalline order making data analysis challenging~\cite{Fraser_a13879}. Striated muscles represent a particulary challenging example system, made up of partially crystalline arrays of actin and myosin containing myofilaments with helical substructures embedded in an amorphous substrate composed of soluble proteins in the cytoplasm, connective tissues, nerves and blood vessels. Consequently, the diffraction signals from the myofilaments resulting in the equatorial and meridional reflections and continuous modulated bands of intensity along the layer lines, are superimposed on a strong amorphous background scattering. There is also a very strong equatorial streak underlying the strong equatorial reflections arising from the hexagonally packed myofilaments. Together, these features prohibit direct application of standard crystallographic approaches motivating the development of data reduction packages specific for muscle diffraction. 

In the 1990s the now disbanded CCP13 project produced a foundational data analysis suite offering a pipeline for analysis of two-dimensional images: determining fiber and detector geometry, background subtraction, integrated layer line intensities and model refinement~\cite{squire2003ccp13_software, shotton1998ccp13_software_dev, denny1995ccp13_updates}. The later FiberFix package~\cite{HeEtAl2007} was intended as a more user-friendly, integrated version of the CCP13 suite that combines core functionality into a single program and offers a GUI. It offered background subtraction using the roving window, radially symmetric and iterative smoothing procedures described below. Neither CCP13 nor Fiberfix are currently supported. Recently, MuscleX~\cite{JiratrakanvongEtAl2024} has been developed as an integrated, open-source software package for X-ray fiber diffraction patterns from striated muscle and other fibrous systems. It builds on and replaces much of FibreFix/CCP13 functionality, especially for muscle and time-resolved work. It offers multiple background subtraction methods, listed below, and provides an interface for visual assessment and parameter tuning. A difficulty for the field is that none of the available algorithms as implemented in CCP13 or MuscleX can provide completely satisfactory background subtracted patterns for entire images. Algorithm and parameter choices for the background subtraction routines are usually guided by qualitative visual assessment by domain experts, motivating the need for standardized, quantitative evaluation criteria to compare and optimize background-removal methods.

\subsection{One-dimensional background subtraction}

Background removal in diffraction analysis is traditionally performed on one-dimensional intensity profiles extracted along directions of interest, most commonly the equator and meridian. In standard practice, diffraction peaks are analyzed individually, and background intensity is estimated by averaging signal in regions adjacent to each peak, which is then subtracted from the integrated peak intensity~\cite{NederKorsunskiy2018}. This approach is simple and effective for isolated reflections, but it depends on careful selection of background regions and does not account for spatial variation in the background.

Another commonly used technique applies the convex hull algorithm~\cite{jarvis1973convex_hull} to one-dimensional intensity profiles to separate diffraction peaks from a smoothly varying background~\cite{lee2019musclex}. While robust to large-scale intensity variations, the convex hull method does not explicitly model background noise, and this can introduce systematic errors in quantitative measurements. For crystalline powder diffraction patterns, a variety of methods estimate a slowly varying smooth curve underneath the sharp peaks~\cite{bruckner2000background, PolarBackground2024, ning2014beads, baek2015baseline, prescher2015dioptas}; however, muscle diffraction peaks are less well defined and are more difficult to isolate from the background.


Parametric background models are also widely used in one-dimensional analyses, especially in crystalline patterns~\cite{toby2013gsas2} and polymer fibers~\cite{grubb1992high_modulus_pe_fiber}. Low-order polynomials, Chebyshev polynomials, or spline functions are commonly employed to represent slowly varying backgrounds, while exponential components may be introduced to model steeper background variations~\cite{rajkumar2007fibrefix}. Although these approaches, when applied to fiber diffraction patterns, are effective for sharp reflections along the equator and meridian, they inherently discard spatial information contained in the two-dimensional shapes of diffraction features. As a result, one-dimensional methods have limitations for analyzing continuous diffraction features such as layer lines, where the two-dimensional shapes may contain important structural information.

\subsection{Two-dimensional background subtraction}

Before applying any background subtraction technique, a sample-free measurement is typically subtracted to remove instrumental artifacts and air scattering. Two-dimensional background subtraction methods operate directly on diffraction patterns and aim to preserve spatial information that is lost in one-dimensional projections.

Some two-dimensional approaches rely on explicit assumptions about the geometry or smoothness of the background. Radial symmetry methods assume that the background intensity is symmetric about the beam center and estimate it by averaging pixel intensities over concentric rings~\cite{rajkumar2007fibrefix}. This method is practical for crystalline powder diffraction where the background is isotropic and can often contain rings or halos~\cite{ashiotis2015pyfai}. While computationally efficient, this assumption breaks down in the presence of anisotropic background features, such as the equatorial streak characteristic of muscle fiber diffraction. Partition-based methods divide the diffraction pattern into local regions, estimate the background within each region, and interpolate between regions to form a continuous background image. Examples include the roving window method~\cite{HeEtAl2007} and the radial convex hull approach, which applies one-dimensional convex hull subtraction to angular projections of the pattern~\cite{lee2019musclex}. Although these methods can adapt to spatially varying backgrounds, their performance depends sensitively on partition size and angular resolution and may introduce artifacts in regions with steep background gradients. In particular, the radial convex hull method tends to perform poorly at high scattering angles, where angular sectors span large regions of the detector.

Other methods treat background subtraction as an image processing problem, exploiting differences in spatial scale or frequency content between background and diffraction features. Iterative low-pass filtering assumes that background variations occur at lower spatial frequencies than diffraction peaks and estimates the background through repeated smoothing~\cite{IvanovaMakowski1998}. Its effectiveness depends strongly on the chosen filter scale and the number of iterations, and weak or low-contrast diffraction features may be partially removed. Morphological filtering techniques, such as white top-hat operations~\cite{GonzalezWoodsDIP4e}, estimate the background using a structuring element and subtract it from the original image. Performance depends on the size of the structuring element relative to feature size, and inappropriate choices may suppress extended or low-intensity diffraction signals. Frequency-domain approaches based on Fourier or wavelet transforms separate image components by spatial frequency. These techniques are effective for crystalline diffraction patterns with sharp features~\cite{PolarBackground2024, WaveletBiomedOptExpress2021, renedecotret2017baseline}, but are less suitable for fiber diffraction, where signal and background overlap in frequency space. Kernel-based rolling-ball methods~\cite{Sternberg1983} similarly perform well for crystalline samples, but are generally less effective for fiber diffraction data.

More recently, data-driven approaches have been explored. Supervised deep learning methods can be trained to perform various image processing tasks, including background subtraction, when sufficient labeled data are available~\cite{archana2024dl_image_processing_review}. However, with fiber diffraction patterns, the lack of ground-truth background and the difficulty of generating representative training datasets limit the practical applicability of such approaches. A neural network has been used to learn muscle fiber diffraction backgrounds based on non-parametric method estimates~\cite{ArangurenCarmona2020}. The model is trained on non-ground truth labels, and it is also not constrained and may alter the pattern, hindering further analysis. Deep learning has also been widely used for function approximation~\cite{devore2021nn_approximation}; however, there is no agreed upon functional form of the background in muscle fiber diffraction patterns, which varies across samples and experimental conditions. Traditionally in machine learning, background subtraction refers to object detection which involves precise edge detection and is often utilized for processing video data~\cite{bouwmans2019dnn_background_subtraction}. The diffraction features that are superimposed on top of the background may be considered the foreground; however, their edges are not well-defined. Background removal in other contexts can also refer to noise removal~\cite{jiang2025efficient_denoising_survey}; in fiber diffraction, noise should be preserved while the high intensity smooth background component should be removed.  In patterns from crystalline systems, where background removal is less of a challenge compared to fiber diffraction, deep learning generally has been used for a variety of tasks, including pattern classification~\cite{aguiar2020crystallographic_prediction, ziletti2018dl_crystal_classification}, labelling~\cite{chavez2025mlexchange_labeling}, interpretation of patterns~\cite{szymanski2021probabilistic_multiphase_xrd} and inpainting of missing data~\cite{chavez2022dl_inpainting_xray,roberts2024dlsia}.

\section{Methodology}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

We evaluate and optimize background subtraction for muscle fiber diffraction patterns using physics-informed metrics, synthetic-signal validation that addresses missing ground truth data and using region-specific masking. An aggregate loss function is used to guide automated parameter search. The experimental design, including algorithm variants and datasets, is described.


\subsection{Evaluation Framework}

An ideal background-subtracted diffraction image (i) preserves diffraction features, (ii) avoids non-physical artifacts, especially extended negative regions indicative of oversubtraction, (iii) leaves a smooth, near-zero residual in background-only regions, and (iv) yields consistent results for images acquired under similar experimental conditions. These objectives motivate the metrics below.


\subsubsection{Quantitative Metrics}

Metrics are defined to capture deviations from the described objectives in a reproducible way.

Oversubtraction is measured by $f_{\mathrm{neg,con}}$, the fraction of negative pixels in connected components larger than a minimum size (e.g.\ 9 pixels), which is ideally zero. Noise tends to create isolated negatives, while oversubtraction creates contiguous regions.

Residual baseline is summarized by $f_B$, the fraction of pixels within a small threshold of zero. The threshold is estimated near the pattern edge, where background scattering is expected to be minimal. We use $1-f_B$ as a penalty when structured background remains.

Background smoothness in masked background regions is measured by

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{S}_{\mathrm{BG}} =
\frac{1}{N} \sum_i \left| I(y_i + 1) - I(y_i) \right|,
\end{equation}
where the summation is performed over rows within masked background regions to exclude known diffraction features. Smooth residual backgrounds produce low values.

\subsubsection{Validation Using Synthetic Structures}

Because experimental diffraction images do not provide ground-truth background, synthetic diffraction features are introduced to evaluate preservation of faint signals after background subtraction (see Section~3.2.2). 

Preservation of synthetic signal is quantified using the mean squared error between the background-subtracted image and a reference image containing only the injected synthetic features, denoted $\mathrm{MSE}_{\mathrm{syn}}$. Ideally, this value is zero, indicating perfect preservation of synthetic signal. 
Overlap with real features may increase this metric.

Oversubtraction of synthetic features is further assessed by measuring the fraction of negative-valued pixels remaining after removing the injected signal, denoted $f_{\mathrm{neg,syn}}$. This metric emphasizes non-physical negative excursions and is ideally zero.

\subsection{Synthetic Signal Validation and Masking}

This subsection describes the masking strategy and synthetic signal design used for objective evaluation of background subtraction performance in the absence of ground-truth background.

\subsubsection{Region-Specific Masking}

Metrics are computed only in background-dominated regions using complementary masks:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textit{Main signal mask:} an annulus excluding beam center and detector edges ($r_{\mathrm{min}}$, $r_{\mathrm{max}}$). This mask excludes regions containing non-representative or saturated signal.
\item \textit{Equator mask:} a rectangle aligned with the equator to treat the intense streak separately. Because the equatorial streak is typically more intense than the surrounding diffuse background, it is treated separately.
\item \textit{Synthetic mask:} rectangles of extent $2\sigma$ around each injected feature.
\item \textit{Real-feature mask:} bands at layer-line positions with widths set from estimated FWHM via perpendicular projections. This mask is used to avoid contamination from real diffraction signal.
\end{itemize}

Together, these masks restrict metric evaluation to regions dominated by background, ensuring that evaluation reflects background subtraction quality rather than signal content.


\subsubsection{Synthetic Signal Validation Design}

To address the central limitation in the lack of ground-truth, we inject synthetic diffraction features before subtraction and measure their preservation afterward to quantify signal loss and oversubtraction.


Synthetic features are modeled using axis-aligned two-dimensional Gaussian functions elongated along the horizontal axis, reflecting the typical shape of diffraction features such as layer lines. The synthetic feature is defined as
\begin{equation}
G(x, y) = A \exp\!\left(
-\frac{(x-x_0)^2}{2\sigma_x^2}
-\frac{(y-y_0)^2}{2\sigma_y^2}
\right),
\end{equation}
where $A$ is the amplitude, $(x_0, y_0)$ is the feature center, and $\sigma_x$ and $\sigma_y$ are the standard deviations along the horizontal and vertical axes, respectively.

The shape parameters are selected automatically for each image to match the scale of real diffraction features. Based on observed correlations in experimental data, $\sigma_x$ and $\sigma_y$ are derived from the average FWHM of prominent reflections, specifically the $I_{1,0}$ equatorial peak and the M1 and M3 meridional reflections. Specifically,
\begin{equation}
\sigma_x = \frac{I_{1,0}}{5} \times \frac{1}{2\sqrt{2\ln2}}, 
\qquad
\sigma_y = \frac{\mathrm{M1}}{20} \times \frac{1}{2\sqrt{2\ln2}}.
\end{equation}


Synthetic amplitudes decay radially as $1/r$, which is a small-angle approximation consistent with Lorentz-type reduction in diffracted intensity with distance from the center of the pattern~\cite{Buerger1940,Denny1995}. The maximum amplitude of synthetic features is chosen to align, at each radius, with the average intensity of real features. For each image, the amplitude is scaled relative to the strongest observed reflection, the $I_{1,0}$ equatorial peak. Setting the maximum synthetic feature amplitude to $A_{\mathrm{max}} = 1\%\, I_{1,0}$ ensures that injected features remain faint while spanning a range of scattering angles.

Features are placed on a regular grid with spacing chosen to avoid known layer-line regions to ensure that synthetic features probe background-dominated regions (Figure~\ref{fig:synthetic_structures}).

\subsection{Optimization Procedure}

This subsection describes how the evaluation metrics are combined into a single objective function, which is minimized to tune each method's parameters.

\subsubsection{Aggregate Loss Function}

Background subtraction quality is quantified by a weighted loss that combines the normalized metrics in Table~\ref{tab:loss_terms}:

\begin{equation}
%\begin{aligned}
L =\;
w_1\, S_{\mathrm{BG}}
+ w_2\, f_{\mathrm{neg,con}}
+ w_3\, f_{\mathrm{neg,syn}}
+ w_4\, \mathrm{MSE}_{\mathrm{syn}}
+ w_5\, \bigl(1 - f_{B}\bigr),
%\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where each metric is normalized by its mean across datasets to place terms on a comparable scale.

The weights $w_i$ encode the relative importance of different physical and algorithmic considerations and may be adjusted to reflect user priorities. We compared equal weighting ($1\!:\!1\!:\!1\!:\!1\!:\!1$) to a domain-informed scheme ($3\!:\!3\!:\!2\!:\!2\!:\!1$) that emphasizes smoothness and oversubtraction penalties over the baseline term.

Visual inspection was used to identify subtle artifacts not captured numerically. Domain-informed weighting performed better than equal weighting. Further adjustment was applied to emphasize penalties for oversubtraction of faint synthetic features while maintaining smooth residual background, and the final weights used in this study were $[w_1,w_2,w_3,w_4,w_5]=[0.4,0.1,0.3,0.1,0.1]$.


No single metric is sufficient to guide optimization, whereas optimization using only the two dominant metrics ($w_1 = w_3 = 0.5$) tends to under-subtract the background, demonstrating the need for a multi-term objective function.

\subsubsection{Parameter Optimization}

Since the background subtraction methods are non-differentiable and parameters are largely discrete, we minimize $L$ using a coordinate-descent strategy in which parameters are optimized sequentially, with all other parameters held fixed at their current best values. Parameters are processed in order of estimated significance. Parameters are optimized within preset bounds and initial values; in this study, optimal values fell within $[0,300]$. These bounds may be adjusted for alternative implementations. Optimization proceeds using a decreasing step-size schedule, with step sizes ranging from 50 to 1 as the loss value improves. After the main coordinate descent loop, the best solution parameters are refined using a local grid search.

Images are processed in parallel using multiprocessing to reduce overall runtime. Additionally, for further computational efficiency, background estimates are computed on downsampled images and subtracted at full resolution; downsampling is applied only when it lowers the loss: G-ILPF, B-ILPF, WTH: $\times 2$; RW: $ \times 4$; CS/RCH/AVG: none (see Section~3.4.1).

Overall, the framework enables objective comparison across datasets, identifies methods that perform well on average, and highlights substantial parameter variability that motivates dataset- or image-adaptive tuning.

\section{Conclusion} 
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%


We presented a quantitative, physics-informed framework that evaluates and optimizes background subtraction in muscle fiber diffraction using reproducible metrics and synthetic-signal validation. Across diverse synchrotron datasets, the framework consistently identified background subtraction strategies that balance artifact suppression, background smoothness, and signal preservation. The best-performing method varies with the analyzed dataset. The results show that no single metric is sufficient in isolation; instead, a composite objective function is required to capture the competing physical and algorithmic constraints inherent to background subtraction. 

A key observation is the substantial variability in optimized parameters across datasets, reflecting differences in background structure due to experimental conditions, detectors, and sample preparation. This variability suggests that fixed, globally tuned parameters are unlikely to be optimal, and that future performance gains will likely require dataset-specific or image-specific adaptation.


\section{Future Work}


Ongoing work includes supporting the integration of the framework into MuscleX~\cite{JiratrakanvongEtAl2024,madhurapantula2026musclexdi} for automatic method and parameter selection and reduce manual intervention. We have also been exploring hybrid approaches that combine parametric modeling of the equatorial streak with non-parametric methods for general background. Given this preliminary work, we plan to use it to train a Neural Network for either background estimation or parameter prediction. Since the framework allows to semi-automatically label data, we would like to use it as a preprocessing step to study time resolved dynamics of patterns and backgrounds and track changes to layer lines over time. Additionally, we plan to submit a manuscript to the Journal of Applied Crystallography describing the framework by the end of the Spring 2026 semester.
