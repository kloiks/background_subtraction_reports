\subsection{Problem Statement}

The separation of signal from background is a fundamental problem in quantitative imaging. In current approaches to background removal for muscle fiber diffraction patterns, operator intervention is typically required to visually assess subtraction quality and to manually adjust algorithm parameters. Such manual processing introduces several challenges. First, weak diffraction signals may be lost due to the large intensity contrast between background and peaks, combined with the presence of noise that can mask low-intensity features. Second, reliance on visual assessment introduces subjectivity, leading to solutions that may be plausible but not optimal. Finally, reproducibility depends on careful documentation and reuse of user-selected parameters.

The absence of a standardized, quantitative evaluation framework limits the ability to objectively compare background subtraction algorithms and impedes systematic parameter optimization. This lack of objective assessment hinders reproducibility and slows methodological progress.

\section{Related Work}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Background removal strategies in diffraction imaging depend strongly on both the dimensionality of the data and the structural characteristics of the sample. In muscle fiber diffraction, background subtraction is commonly performed either on one-dimensional intensity profiles extracted from the data or directly on two-dimensional diffraction patterns. This section reviews representative approaches in both categories and highlights their limitations in the context of biological fiber diffraction.

\subsection{One-dimensional background subtraction}

Background removal in diffraction analysis is traditionally performed on one-dimensional intensity profiles extracted along directions of interest, most commonly the equator and meridian. In standard practice, diffraction peaks are analyzed individually, and background intensity is estimated by averaging signal in regions adjacent to each peak, which is then subtracted from the integrated peak intensity~\cite{NederKorsunskiy2018}. This approach is simple and effective for isolated reflections, but depends on careful selection of background regions and does not account for spatial variation in the background.

Another commonly used technique applies the convex hull algorithm to one-dimensional intensity profiles in order to separate diffraction peaks from a smoothly varying background~\cite{lee2019musclex}. While robust to large-scale intensity variations, the convex hull method does not explicitly model background noise and can introduce systematic errors in quantitative measurements. For crystalline powder diffraction patterns, a variety of methods estimate a smooth curve underneath the sharp peaks~\cite{bruckner2000background, PolarBackground2024, ning2014beads, baek2015baseline, prescher2015dioptas}; however, muscle diffraction peaks are less defined and overlap with the background.

Parametric background models are also widely used in one-dimensional analyses, especially in crystalline patterns~\cite{toby2013gsas2} and polymer fibers~\cite{grubb1992high_modulus_pe_fiber}. Low-order polynomials, Chebyshev polynomials, or spline functions are commonly employed to represent slowly varying backgrounds, while exponential components may be introduced to model steeper background variations~\cite{rajkumar2007fibrefix}. Although these approaches are effective in fiber diffraction for sharp reflections along the equator and meridian, they inherently discard spatial information contained in the two-dimensional shapes of diffraction features. As a result, one-dimensional methods provide limited access to continuous diffraction features such as layer lines, which contain important structural information.

\subsection{Two-dimensional background subtraction}

Two-dimensional background subtraction methods operate directly on diffraction patterns and aim to preserve spatial information that is lost in one-dimensional projections. Before applying any background subtraction technique, a sample-free measurement is typically subtracted to remove instrumental artifacts and air scattering.

Some two-dimensional approaches rely on explicit assumptions about the geometry or smoothness of the background. Radial symmetry methods assume that the background intensity is symmetric about the beam center and estimate it by averaging pixel intensities over concentric rings~\cite{rajkumar2007fibrefix}. While computationally efficient, this assumption breaks down in the presence of anisotropic background features, such as the equatorial streak characteristic of muscle fiber diffraction. This method is practical for crystalline powder diffraction where the background is isotopic and can often contain rings or halos~\cite{ashiotis2015pyfai}. Partition-based methods divide the diffraction pattern into local regions, estimate the background within each region, and interpolate between regions to form a continuous background image. Examples include the roving window method implemented in the CCP13 software suite~\cite{HeEtAl2007} and the radial convex hull approach, which applies one-dimensional convex hull subtraction to angular projections of the pattern~\cite{lee2019musclex}. Although these methods can adapt to spatially varying backgrounds, their performance depends sensitively on partition size and angular resolution and may introduce artifacts in regions with steep background gradients. In particular, the radial convex hull method tends to perform poorly at high scattering angles, where angular sectors span large regions of the detector.

Other methods treat background subtraction as an image processing problem, exploiting differences in spatial scale or frequency content between background and diffraction features. Iterative low-pass filtering assumes that background variations occur at lower spatial frequencies than diffraction peaks and estimates the background through repeated smoothing~\cite{IvanovaMakowski1998}. Its effectiveness depends strongly on the chosen filter scale and the number of iterations, and weak or low-contrast diffraction features may be partially removed. Morphological filtering techniques, such as white top-hat operations~\cite{GonzalezWoodsDIP4e}, estimate the background using a structuring element and subtract it from the original image. Performance depends on the size of the structuring element relative to feature size, and inappropriate choices may suppress extended or low-intensity diffraction signals. Frequency-domain approaches based on Fourier or wavelet transforms separate image components by spatial frequency~\cite{PolarBackground2024, WaveletBiomedOptExpress2021, renedecotret2017baseline}. These techniques are effective for crystalline diffraction patterns with sharp features, but are less suitable for fiber diffraction, where signal and background overlap in frequency space. Kernel-based rolling-ball methods~\cite{Sternberg1983} similarly perform well for crystalline samples, but are generally less effective for fiber diffraction data.

More recently, data-driven approaches have been explored. Supervised deep learning methods can be trained to perform various image processing tasks, including background subtraction, when sufficient labeled data are available~\cite{archana2024dl_image_processing_review}. However, in diffraction imaging, the lack of ground-truth background and the difficulty of generating representative training datasets limit the practical applicability of such approaches. Deep learning has also been widely used for function approximation~\cite{devore2021nn_approximation}; however, there is no agreed upon functional form of the background in muscle fiber, which varies across samples and experimental conditions. A neural network has been used to learn muscle fiber diffraction backgrounds based on non-parametric method estimates~\cite{ArangurenCarmona2020}, where the labels used are not ground truth and the model is not constrained and may alter the pattern, hindering further analysis. Traditionally in machine learning, background subtraction in image data can refer to object detection which is considered the foreground and the rest of the image is the background; this task involves precise edge detection and is often utilized for processing video data~\cite{bouwmans2019dnn_background_subtraction}. The diffraction features that are superimposed on top of the background may be considered the foreground; however, their edges are not well-defined. Background removal in other contexts can also refer to noise removal~\cite{elad2023imagedenoisingdeeplearning}. In fiber diffraction, the smooth background component should be removed while noise is preserved. Therefore, neither tasks are directly applicable. In crystalline patterns, where background removal is less of a challenge compared to fiber diffraction, deep learning has been used for alternative tasks, such as classification and interpretation of patterns~\cite{szymanski2021probabilistic_multiphase_xrd, ziletti2018dl_crystal_classification, aguiar2020crystallographic_prediction}.

\subsection{Available software}
There are a few pieces of software specialized for fiber diffraction. CCP13 is a foundational but now legacy suite foced on fiber and solution scattering analysis from non-crystalline materials~\cite{squire2003new, squire2003ccp13_software, denny1995ccp13_updates}.
It offers a pipeline from processing input 2D images, determining fiber and detector geometry, background subtraction, to integrated layer line intensities and model refinement~\cite{shotton1998ccp13_software_dev}. FiberFix is a more user-friendly, integrated version of the CCP13 suite that combines core functionality into a single program and offers a GUI~\cite{rajkumar2005fibrefix}. It offers background subtraction using the roving window, radially symmetric and iterative smoothing procedures decsribed above~\cite{HeEtAl2007, fibrefix_diamond}. MuscleX is an integrated, open-source software for X-ray fiber diffraction patterns from striated muscle and other fibrous systems. It builds on and replaces much of FibreFix/CCP13 functionality, especially for muscle and time-resolved work~\cite{JiratrakanvongEtAl2024}. It offers multiple background subtraction methods, listed above and provides a user-friendly interface for visual assessment and parameter tuning. 

Algorithm and parameter choices are usually guided by qualitative visual assessment by domain experts, motivating the need for standardized, quantitative evaluation criteria to compare and optimize background-removal methods.

\subsubsection{Region-Specific Masking}

% Accurate calculation of evaluation metrics requires separating regions containing diffraction signal from those dominated by background or artifacts. To achieve this, multiple complementary masks are applied.

% Regions containing non-representative or saturated signal are excluded using a main signal mask. The mask is defined as an annulus with inner radius $r_{\mathrm{min}}$ and outer radius $r_{\mathrm{max}}$.

Because the equatorial streak is typically more intense than the surrounding diffuse background, it is treated separately.

% Regions containing synthetic diffraction features are identified using a synthetic feature mask. Around each injected synthetic structure, a rectangular area extending $2\sigma$ in each direction is masked, where $\sigma$ denotes the standard deviation of the synthetic feature.

% To avoid contamination from real diffraction signal, regions where genuine diffraction features are expected are excluded using a real feature mask. Layer line regions are masked using horizontal bands centered at known layer line positions. The width of each band is determined by estimating the full width at half maximum (FWHM) of the corresponding layer line using perpendicular intensity projections.




Metrics are computed only in background-dominated regions using complementary masks:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textit{Main signal mask:} annulus excluding beam center and detector edges ($r_{\mathrm{min}}$, $r_{\mathrm{max}}$). This mask excludes regions containing non-representative or saturated signal.
\item \textit{Equator mask:} rectangle aligned with the equator to treat the intense streak separately. Because the equatorial streak is typically more intense than the surrounding diffuse background, it is treated separately.
\item \textit{Synthetic mask:} rectangles of extent $2\sigma$ around each injected feature.
\item \textit{Real-feature mask:} bands at layer-line positions with widths set from estimated FWHM via perpendicular projections. This mask is used to avoid contamination from real diffraction signal.
\end{itemize}

Together, these masks restrict metric evaluation to regions dominated by background, ensuring that evaluation reflects background subtraction quality rather than signal content.


\subsubsection{Synthetic Signal Validation Design}

% A central challenge in evaluating background subtraction for diffraction images is the absence of ground-truth background. To address this limitation, synthetic diffraction features resembling real signal are injected into the images prior to background subtraction. Preservation of these features after subtraction provides a controlled measure of signal loss and oversubtraction.

To address the central limitation in the lack of ground-truth, we inject synthetic diffraction features before subtraction and measure their preservation afterward to quantify signal loss and oversubtraction.


Synthetic features are modeled using axis-aligned two-dimensional Gaussian functions elongated along the horizontal axis, reflecting the typical shape of diffraction features such as layer lines. The synthetic feature is defined as
\begin{equation}
G(x, y) = A \exp\!\left(
-\frac{(x-x_0)^2}{2\sigma_x^2}
-\frac{(y-y_0)^2}{2\sigma_y^2}
\right),
\end{equation}
where $A$ is the amplitude, $(x_0, y_0)$ is the feature center, and $\sigma_x$ and $\sigma_y$ are the standard deviations along the horizontal and vertical axes, respectively.
% This formulation corresponds to an axis-aligned Gaussian without rotational components.

The shape parameters are selected automatically for each image to match the scale of real diffraction features. Based on observed correlations in experimental data, $\sigma_x$ and $\sigma_y$ are derived from the average FWHM of prominent reflections, specifically the $I_{1,0}$ equatorial peak and the M1 and M3 meridional reflections. Specifically,
\begin{equation}
\sigma_x = \frac{I_{1,0}}{5} \times \frac{1}{2\sqrt{2\ln2}}, 
\qquad
\sigma_y = \frac{\mathrm{M1}}{20} \times \frac{1}{2\sqrt{2\ln2}}.
\end{equation}

% The radial decay of synthetic feature intensity follows a $1/r$ model, where $r$ is the distance from the pattern center. This choice reflects the scattering geometry of X-ray diffraction and approximates the Lorentz correction, which for equatorial and meridional reflections can be expressed as $L \propto 1/\sin 2\theta$~\cite{Buerger1940}. At small scattering angles, $\theta$ is approximately proportional to $r$, making a $1/r$ decay a reasonable approximation~\cite{Denny1995}.

Synthetic amplitudes decay radially as $1/r$, which is a small-angle approximation consistent with Lorentz-type behavior~\cite{Buerger1940,Denny1995}. The maximum amplitude of synthetic features is chosen to align, at each radius, with the average intensity of real features. For each image, the amplitude is scaled relative to the strongest observed reflection, the $I_{1,0}$ equatorial peak. Setting the maximum synthetic feature amplitude to $A_{\mathrm{max}} = 1\%\, I_{1,0}$ ensures that injected features remain faint while spanning a range of scattering angles.

The sampling frequency $(f_x, f_y)$ controls placement density and is chosen to avoid overlap with known diffraction features. Layer line positions are explicitly accounted for to ensure that synthetic features probe background-dominated regions.

\subsubsection{Aggregate Loss Function}

% Background subtraction quality is quantified using a weighted aggregate loss function that combines the normalized metrics defined in the evaluation framework (Table~\ref{tab:loss_terms}). The objective function is constructed to balance physically meaningful constraints, artifact suppression, and preservation of faint diffraction signal. It is defined as

Background subtraction quality is quantified by a weighted loss that combines the normalized metrics in Table~\ref{tab:loss_terms}:

\begin{equation}
%\begin{aligned}
L =\;
w_1\, S_{\mathrm{BG}}
+ w_2\, f_{\mathrm{neg,con}}
+ w_3\, f_{\mathrm{neg,syn}}
+ w_4\, \mathrm{MSE}_{\mathrm{syn}}
+ w_5\, \bigl(1 - f_{B}\bigr),
%\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
% where each metric is normalized by its average value over the dataset prior to aggregation, ensuring comparable scale during optimization.

% The weights $w_i$ encode the relative importance of different physical and algorithmic considerations and may be adjusted to reflect user priorities. We initially compare two weighting strategies: equal weighting, with $w_1 : w_2 : w_3 : w_4 : w_5 = 1 : 1 : 1 : 1 : 1$, and domain-informed weighting, with $w_1 : w_2 : w_3 : w_4 : w_5 = 3 : 3 : 2 : 2 : 1$. In the latter case, higher weights are assigned to metrics enforcing direct physical constraints, such as background smoothness and suppression of connected negative regions. Metrics related to synthetic signal preservation receive moderate weight, while the baseline background term is weighted more lightly, as it primarily reflects the degree of overall background cleanup.

The weights $w_i$ encode the relative importance of different physical and algorithmic considerations and may be adjusted to reflect user priorities. We compared equal weighting ($1\!:\!1\!:\!1\!:\!1\!:\!1$) to a domain-informed scheme ($3\!:\!3\!:\!2\!:\!2\!:\!1$) that emphasizes smoothness and oversubtraction penalties over the baseline term.

Based on visual comparison, domain-informed weighting consistently produced slightly better results than equal weighting. \subsubsection{Parameter Optimization}

% The aggregate loss function is minimized to identify optimal parameter values for each background subtraction method. % The aggregate loss function is minimized to identify optimal parameter values for each background subtraction method. Because these methods typically involve non-differentiable operations and discrete parameter choices (e.g.\ window size or number of iterations), gradient-based optimization is not well suited. Instead, a direct search strategy that relies solely on function evaluations is used, despite higher computational cost. We apply a coordinate descent strategy in which parameters are optimized sequentially, with all other parameters held fixed at their current best values. Input parameters are either integer-valued or exhibit no qualitative sensitivity to fractional changes. For each algorithm, predefined initial values and parameter bounds are specified. Across the datasets considered here, optimized parameter values for all methods fall within the range $[0, 300]$, although these bounds may be adjusted for alternative implementations.

Since the background subtraction methods are non-differentiable and parameters are largely discrete, we minimize $L$ using coordinate-descent strategy in which parameters are optimized sequentially, with all other parameters held fixed at their current best values. Parameters are processed in order of estimated significance. Parameters are optimized within preset bounds/initial values; in this study, optimal values fell within $[0,300]$. These bounds may be adjusted for alternative implementations. Optimization proceeds using a decreasing step-size schedule, with step sizes ranging from 50 to 1 as the loss value improves. After the main coordinate descent loop, the best solution parameters are refined using a local grid search.

\section{Conclusion} 
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

% We presented a quantitative, physics-informed framework for the evaluation and optimization of background subtraction methods in muscle fiber diffraction images. By translating qualitative expectations of successful background removal into objective, reproducible metrics, the framework enables systematic comparison of algorithms and data-driven selection of method parameters. The introduction of synthetic diffraction features provides a practical mechanism for assessing preservation of faint signal in the absence of ground-truth background, addressing a fundamental challenge in experimental diffraction analysis.

% Across diverse synchrotron datasets, the framework consistently identified background subtraction strategies that balance artifact suppression, background smoothness, and signal preservation. Iterative low-pass filtering methods achieved the best overall performance, while the mean-value baseline confirmed that structured background modeling is essential for reliable analysis. The results also show that no single metric is sufficient in isolation; instead, a composite objective function is required to capture the competing physical and algorithmic constraints inherent to background subtraction.

% A key observation is the substantial variability in optimized parameters across datasets, reflecting differences in background structure due to experimental conditions, detectors, and sample preparation. This variability suggests that fixed, globally tuned parameters are unlikely to be optimal, and that future performance gains will likely require dataset-specific or image-specific adaptation.

We presented a quantitative, physics-informed framework that evaluates and optimizes background subtraction in muscle fiber diffraction using reproducible metrics and synthetic-signal validation. Across diverse synchrotron datasets, the framework consistently identified methods that balance background smoothness, artifact suppression, and faint-signal preservation. A key observation is the substantial variability in optimized parameters across images, motivating dataset- or image-adaptive tuning.


% While the primary focus of this work was on non-parametric background removal, exploratory experiments with parametric models for the general background and equatorial streak highlighted complementary strengths, including compact parameterization and noise robustness. These results suggest that hybrid or adaptive approaches combining parametric modeling with data-driven evaluation may offer further improvements.

% Overall, the proposed framework establishes an objective and reproducible foundation for background subtraction in muscle fiber diffraction. By enabling systematic evaluation and parameter optimization, it supports more reliable quantitative analysis and provides a basis for future developments in adaptive and hybrid background modeling strategies, both in muscle diffraction and in other imaging modalities characterized by complex, non-uniform backgrounds.
